## 1. The Stanford wall

The founder is a Stanford PhD student until ~2029. Universities (Stanford via its patent
policy and the SU-18 agreement students/researchers sign) typically claim inventions that are
(a) conceived or first reduced to practice in the course of sponsored research, or (b) made
with more-than-incidental use of university resources. Federal funding (DOE/NSF — note the
NSF grant on the HSX sensor paper) adds Bayh-Dole obligations on anything within the funded
project scope. Therefore, for every proposed invention:

- **Independence test (answer all four in the disclosure):** (1) Is the concept outside the
  scope of the founder's funded PhD research and any signed sponsored-project statement of
  work? (2) Can it be conceived and reduced to practice using ONLY personal time, personal
  funds, personal equipment, and non-Stanford facilities (home bench / rented makerspace /
  commercial services)? (3) Does it avoid Stanford confidential information and unpublished
  group results? (4) Does it avoid SNF tools, group lab equipment, Stanford compute, and
  Stanford-administered purchases entirely? Any "no" → **BLOCKED-PENDING-COUNSEL**, excluded
  from the filing roadmap, listed in the audit table with the failing prong.
- **Evidence protocol (goes into the roadmap):** dated personal notebook entries, personal-
  account repo commits, personal-card receipts, photos/videos of home-bench reduction to
  practice, no Stanford email for anything invention-related. The point is a clean paper
  trail if ownership is ever examined.
- **PhD-adjacent ideas are the danger zone.** GaN Hall sensing, stellarator diagnostics, and
  battery magnetic imaging are presumptively inside the research scope — inventions there are
  BLOCKED-PENDING-COUNSEL by default. The C12/C10 lanes (winding machines, converters) are
  outside the dissertation topic, which is exactly why they can pass the wall — but each
  disclosure must still argue it individually.
- **Counsel checkpoint:** before the first real filing, the roadmap must schedule (i) reading
  the founder's actual signed SU-18/appointment/fellowship terms, and (ii) an optional
  Stanford OTL confirmation of non-claim for borderline items. This mission cannot make that
  determination; it can only design inventions that maximize the odds.

## 3. File-before-publish (the rule that interacts with the Round-1 plan)

- **US:** first-inventor-to-file; a 1-year grace period exists for the inventor's own
  disclosures (35 U.S.C. 102(b)(1)) — a fallback, not a plan.
- **China:** **no general grace period.** The narrow 6-month exceptions (State-Council-
  recognized exhibitions, prescribed academic conferences, disclosure without consent,
  public-health emergency) do **not** cover ordinary journal or IEEE publication. Publishing
  the tabletop-NI-rig benchmark in SUST/IEEE TAS before filing kills CN novelty.
- **Europe:** effectively no grace period.
- **Consequence (binding):** every disclosure carries a publish/file sequencing line, and the
  IP roadmap re-orders the Round-1 validation-experiment publications so that any publication
  describing a claimed invention is preceded by at least a US provisional whose text actually
  supports the later claims (a thin provisional protects nothing — the roadmap must say what
  the provisional needs to contain).
- **Foreign-filing license:** inventions made in the US require a license (35 U.S.C. 184)
  before first filing abroad — filing US-first (provisional counts) is the standard-safe
  route; note it, don't improvise around it. If any co-inventor contributes from China,
  flag CN confidentiality-examination (保密审查) as a counsel question.

## 5. Patent vs trade secret (decide per invention, in the disclosure)

Patent well: mechanisms, fixtures, sensing arrangements, converter topologies, control
architectures observable in a shipped product (reverse-engineerable → secret fails).
Keep secret: process recipe databases (tension→Rc maps), calibration datasets, yield
statistics, software internals not observable at the interface — these are the data flywheel;
filing them teaches competitors. Default rule: **if a competitor could learn it from buying
one unit, patent it; if it only exists in accumulated data, trade-secret it** (and the
roadmap's trade-secret register states handling: access control, contracts, marking).

## 6. Inventorship & assignment

Sole-inventor filings unless a person genuinely contributed to conception (then they are a
legal co-inventor — plan around it, don't paper over it). File as individual (or personal
LLC if formed); execute assignments to NewCo at incorporation; the roadmap includes this
step explicitly so nothing is orphaned in 2029.
