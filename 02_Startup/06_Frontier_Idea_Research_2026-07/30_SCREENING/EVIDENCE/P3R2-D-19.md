# P3R2-D-19 — Demand/Competitor Evidence

Concept: Distributed composite-flywheel kinetic buffers for off-grid AI power plants -- containerized
magnetic-bearing flywheel strings absorbing GPU-cluster power swings so gas turbines/grids see
smooth load. Primary market: US (industrial). Primary lane L16, secondary L02/L08.

**Binding pre-promotion condition (fix_applied_notes, 2026-07-13 FIX):** the 2026-27 precompany plan
must be "FIX-hardened" -- an instrumented load-spectrum study with a datacenter partner to *measure*
(not assert) the microcycle buffering niche, plus quantified cycle-life economics vs. Piller-class
synchronous machines and vs. BESS at measured microcycle rates. The CN content (merged from B-18) is
explicitly a license-note only, no CN beachhead. Evaluated first because it shapes what counts as
satisfied demand evidence.

## 1. Demand

**Primary (buyer-side documentation):** Bergen Engines / Langley Holdings press release,
2025-11-26 (`P3R2-D-19-S01`, fetched, bergenengines.com): confirms the named 400MW US East Coast
off-grid AI data-center power plant, with **E-Finity Distributed Generation as EPC/O&M provider**
(the actual buyer entity), 36 x 11.2MW Bergen gas engines, and explicit deployment of **Piller's
SHIELDX Dynamic Power Stabilization units** (flywheel-based mechanical inertia + bi-directional
active power modules) specifically to absorb sub-second AI load-step transients up to 30%. This is
real, dated, named-buyer procurement evidence for exactly the architecture class (flywheel-based
dynamic power stabilization for off-grid AI power plants) the seed proposes -- the project also grew
4x from an originally-conceived 100MW plant, underscoring real and growing scale.

**Independent second demand source:** GE Vernova Q1-2026 earnings release, 2026-04-22
(`P3R2-D-19-S02`, fetched, gevernova.com, company-filing-grade primary source): Electrification
segment booked **$2.4 billion in Q1-2026 equipment orders to support data centers alone -- more than
all of 2025 combined.** This is independent (different company, different product category --
substations/switchgear/HVDC, not flywheels specifically) confirmation that the broader
datacenter-power-buildout wave the seed's demand thesis depends on is real and accelerating, not
merely a single anecdote.

**Verdict: demand is real, named, and current.** The specific SHIELDX/Bergen precedent is the
strongest single piece of evidence found in this entire assignment -- an actual buyer has already
procured flywheel-based dynamic stabilization for exactly this application.

## 2. Competitors

- **Piller** (Hannover, Germany; owned by **Langley Holdings**, UK) is the incumbent of record on
  the flagship named project (`P3R2-D-19-S01`). Piller's US manufacturing lineage runs through its
  2016 acquisition of **Active Power** (Austin, TX) -- DataCenterKnowledge, 2016-10-03
  (`P3R2-D-19-S04`, fetched): confirms Piller/Langley bought the financially distressed Active
  Power specifically for its flywheel-based UPS technology and committed to keep manufacturing in
  Austin. Net: the single named incumbent on the flagship project is a European-owned but
  US-manufacturing-rooted company with a decade-plus flywheel heritage -- a credible, entrenched
  incumbent, not a greenfield opportunity.
- **Stornetic GmbH** (Jülich, Germany; subsidiary of **Enrichment Technology Limited**)
  (`P3R2-D-19-S03`, fetched, stornetic.com): makes **composite carbon-fiber flywheels** ("EnWheel")
  derived from 40 years of uranium-enrichment-centrifuge manufacturing heritage -- technologically
  very close to the seed's proposed composite-rotor/AMB architecture. Confirmed grid-stabilization
  installations in Germany (EDF France, KIT Germany, Nippon Koei Japan) but **no datacenter-specific
  deployment was found this session** -- a credible European competitor that has not yet entered the
  AI-datacenter niche specifically.
- Beacon Power, Amber Kinetics, and VYCON were also identified via WebSearch as active US flywheel
  vendors, but Amber Kinetics is a long-duration (hours-scale) design not optimized for
  millisecond-response power-quality buffering, and Beacon Power/VYCON were not independently
  fetched this session (context only, not formally sourced).

## 3. Price signals and arithmetic

- No fetched, load-bearing $/kWh or $/kW figure for AMB composite-rotor flywheels was obtained this
  session (a market-aggregator range of ~$400-$6,000/kWh and ~$600-$1,000/kW for UPS-class flywheels
  was found via WebSearch only, not independently fetched -- **T3-equivalent, flagged as
  context/assumption, not a formal accepted source**).
- **[Assumption, wide uncertainty]:** applying that aggregator range to the seed's proposed
  250-500kW / 10-25MJ (2.8-6.9kWh) module implies a raw system cost on the order of **$1,100-
  $41,000 per module** at generic industrial-flywheel pricing -- but a specialty composite-rotor,
  active-magnetic-bearing, containerized, millisecond-response unit (closer to Stornetic's
  EnWheel/DuraStor class than to a generic steel-rotor UPS flywheel) plausibly commands a
  substantial premium over this range; no specialty-AMB-flywheel price point was found this session,
  so this is flagged as a real pricing gap rather than resolved.
- **Installed-base scenario [assumption, not fact]:** if NVIDIA's Kyber/800VDC architecture
  (targeted for full-scale production in 2027, `P3R2-D-19-S05`, fetched, developer.nvidia.com)
  drives >1MW/rack GW-scale campuses, and even a modest fraction (order-of-magnitude
  10-20%, a scenario not a forecast) of new off-grid/weak-grid AI campuses through the early 2030s
  adopt kinetic buffering at feeder/PDU level, installed base could scale from the single flagship
  project today to hundreds of modules across the US by the mid-2030s -- a wide range that should be
  treated as a scenario, not a sized market.

## 6. 2030 timing

1. **Primary/company-schedule trigger:** NVIDIA Kyber rack architecture / 800VDC full-scale
   production targeted for **2027** (`P3R2-D-19-S05`, fetched, developer.nvidia.com, published
   2025-05-20), with >1MW/rack power density (up from a 200kW baseline) -- a concrete, dated,
   industry-roadmap trigger squarely inside the 2028-2035 window (just before it, actually inside
   the founder's 2026-2029 pre-company period, meaning the DC-native buffering socket the seed
   depends on should already exist by company launch in 2030).
2. **Independent second trigger:** GE Vernova Q1-2026 order surge, $2.4B/quarter for datacenter
   electrification alone (`P3R2-D-19-S02`, fetched, 2026-04-22) -- confirms the underlying buildout
   wave is accelerating now, not a distant forecast.

**Verdict: window is open now and persists at least through the early 2030s** as GW-scale AI campus
buildouts continue; the seed's own timing risk (BESS cost declines or rack-level software/capacitance
smoothing shrinking the niche to harmonics-scale) remains the correct primary risk to watch, not a
demand-existence problem.
