## 1. Demand

**No PRIMARY buyer/procurement source was found.** Unlike A-13/A-14/A-21/A-22, this idea's customers (chip packagers,
AI accelerator vendors) do not run public tenders for thermal interface materials — TIM is a mid-supply-chain
material input, not a line item in any public procurement record. This is a structural feature of the niche, not a
search failure, but it means demand rests on inference:

- **Technical trigger (not itself demand evidence):** NVIDIA's own roadmap shows chip-level TDP already at
  1,000-2,300W (Rubin Max-P) and rack density moving from ~120-130kW (2026, NVL144) to ~600kW (2027, Kyber/NVL576)
  with "complete liquid immersion, zero fans" [new:P3R2-A-16-S01]. This confirms the physical problem is real and
  accelerating faster than the idea's own 2026 framing suggested ("push toward 1,000 W/cm2 by the early 2030s" — the
  chip-level number is already there in 2026).
- **Government R&D co-funding (indirect demand signal):** DOE's COOLERCHIPS program funded 15 projects ($40M total,
  including NVIDIA at $5M and JetCool at $1.27M) in 2023, with ARPA-E targeting proof-of-concept milestones in 1H2026
  [new:P3R2-A-16-S02]. This shows sustained institutional recognition of the extreme-cooling problem but is R&D
  co-funding, not a TIM buyer commitment, and the award itself is three years old.
- **Substitution risk (works against demand for TIM specifically):** the same rack-density trend is being addressed
  via architecture — full liquid immersion, direct-to-chip cold plates, and micro-convective jet impingement (JetCool,
  acquired by Flex in 2024, atlas-confirmed [atlas:L14-045]) — rather than via better TIM. A generational shift to
  liquid-metal-in-the-loop or direct bonding could bypass the TIM-improvement wedge entirely.

**Verdict: G1 — FAIL on strict "named buyer/procurement" criterion; UNCERTAIN overall.** State this plainly: there is
no first-party evidence of a chip packager or accelerator vendor committing to buy an advanced TIM system. Demand is
inferred from a real and accelerating technical trigger plus government R&D interest, not from buyer behavior. This is
exactly why the idea's own judges attached a 2028 anchor-packager-LOI gate rather than promoting on demand evidence
alone — that gate is the correct instrument, and it remains unmet.

**China:** A real, standards-backed domestic liquid-metal TIM industry exists — China's national standard GB/T

academic origins at the CAS Institute of Physics (Liu Jing's team, 2012) and commercial entrants like Suzhou
Techinnotech [new:P3R2-A-16-S05]. China is not a covered market per this engagement's geography rules, but this
confirms Chinese firms are real product-level competitors in liquid-metal TIM technology, not just a raw-material
chokepoint.

**Japan/Taiwan/Korea:** named as secondary markets in the idea record (TW/KR/JP); no new named competitor found this

- **Bottom-up arithmetic attempt (labeled assumption, wide uncertainty):**
  - **Fact:** Rubin-class AI accelerator packages are shipping at 1,000-2,300W chip-level TDP in 2026
    [new:P3R2-A-16-S01].
  - **Assumption:** NVIDIA-class data-center GPU shipments run in the low millions of units/year by 2027-2028 (public
    industry knowledge, not independently sourced this session) and an advanced TIM/reliability-engineering premium
    might command $5-$50 per package versus commodity TIM (assumption, no vendor pricing found).
  - This would imply a addressable premium-TIM revenue pool of **very roughly $5M-$150M/year** across the AI
    accelerator segment alone by 2027-2028 — an extremely wide, low-confidence range that should not be treated as a
    validated TAM. The idea's own capital ask ($4-10M V1) is modest relative to even the low end of this range,
    which is a mild positive signal but does not substitute for the missing buyer-commitment evidence in §1.
- Gallium raw-material cost signal (input-side, not TIM-product pricing): Rotterdau gallium spot price reached
  $687/kg by May 2025, a 150% increase over pre-export-control levels [new:P3R2-A-16-S03] — a real, recent, and
  volatile input-cost data point for any gallium-based liquid-metal formulation.

## 4. Certification and export constraints for a 2030 launch

**This is the most significant new finding for A-16.** China supplies 98% of global primary gallium (USGS 2024);
Beijing imposed a functional export ban to the US in December 2024 with extraterritorial enforcement, and
~85% of DoD gallium-containing defense supply chains include at least one Chinese supplier [new:P3R2-A-16-S03]. A
bilateral truce suspended the ban from November 9, 2025 through **November 27, 2026**, with explicit "no long-term
guarantees beyond" that date, and third-country transshipment (Hong Kong, Singapore) restrictions remaining in force
even during the suspension [new:P3R2-A-16-S04].

**This is a critical, currently-unaddressed supply-chain risk for a gallium-based liquid-metal TIM company**: the
core raw-material input sits behind a Chinese export-control chokepoint that is scheduled to potentially re-tighten
just before the 2026-2029 precompany build window closes, and squarely inside it either way. A founder pursuing this
idea should treat gallium sourcing/stockpiling/alternative-alloy strategy (e.g., bismuth/indium/tin-based alloys, which
several vendors already blend to reduce pure-gallium dependence) as a first-order de-risking workstream, not an
afterthought — this is a materially bigger and more concrete risk than the idea's own `timing_window_risk` field
currently captures.

No other significant export-control or certification regime applies (TIM is not a controlled dual-use item by
itself).

## 6. 2030 timing

**Timing source 1 (technical/industry roadmap, inside 2028-2035 window):** NVIDIA's own rack-scale roadmap —
600kW/rack "Kyber" systems in 2027, ~1MW-class racks expected around 2028 (Feynman architecture per trade reporting)
[new:P3R2-A-16-S01] — is a concrete, dated technical trigger for extreme-flux packaging.

**Timing source 2 (regulatory/supply-chain, dated):** the China gallium export-ban suspension expires **November 27,
2026** [new:P3R2-A-16-S04] — a hard, dated risk event that precedes the idea's own 2028 FIX-gate checkpoint and
therefore should be monitored as an input-cost/availability risk before the anchor-packager LOI decision point is
even reached.

**Verdict: the technical window for extreme-flux TIM appears to OPEN on schedule or even earlier than assumed (chip
TDP already exceeds the seed's 2030s framing in 2026), but PERSISTENCE of a merchant-TIM opportunity through 2030 is
genuinely uncertain** given (a) the missing buyer-commitment evidence, (b) real substitution risk from direct-liquid/
immersion architectures, and (c) an unresolved upstream gallium supply-chain risk. This is the lowest-confidence idea
of the five reviewed, consistent with its own "confidence: low" rating — the evidence found here does not raise that
confidence level.
