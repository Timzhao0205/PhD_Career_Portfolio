# P3R2-D-16 — Demand/Competitor Evidence

Concept: Merchant 100 kWe-class space closed-Brayton power conversion unit and high-voltage PMAD for
fission surface power (FSP) -- the converter layer for the reactor decade. Primary market: US
(government/NASA-DOE). Primary lane L09.

**Binding pre-promotion condition (fix_applied_notes, 2026-07-13 FIX):** idea is "HELD AS OPTION" --
studies/SBIR only, no hardware capex until FSP downselect/flight dates are contracted, with a 2028
checkpoint gating any move to a 10kWe turboalternator rig; parallel-unit architecture must be
validated with >=2 concept teams expressing written subcontract interest before proceeding.
Evaluated first because it governs the gate result.

## 1. Demand

**Primary/official government directive:** PowerMag, "White House Launches Space Nuclear
Initiative, Sets Timeline for Lunar Reactors" (`P3R2-D-16-S01`, fetched, published 2026-04-16,
reporting the 2026-04-14 memorandum): National Space Technology Memorandum-3 (NSTM-3) formally
launches the National Initiative for American Space Nuclear Power, directs NASA to develop a
mid-power (>=20kWe) lunar surface reactor for a 2030 launch and a higher-power (>=100kWe) reactor
for the 2030s, and requires a downselect to no more than two designs within one year of the
memorandum (i.e., by ~April 2027). This directly corroborates the seed's `L09-033`-cited "100kWe"
and "2030s" claims with an independently fetched primary government source.

**Independent second demand source (government funding, not just directive):** SpacePolicyOnline,
"Great News for NASA in the House-Senate FY2026 Appropriations Report" (`P3R2-D-16-S02`, fetched,
2026-01-05): Congress appropriated **$250 million for Fission Surface Power in FY2026** specifically
to support a 2030 lunar reactor, rejecting the administration's broader ~24% NASA cut proposal.
This is a real, dated, primary appropriations action -- money moving, not just a policy memo --
independently confirming the program is funded, not merely directed.

**Historical baseline (context, not counted toward 2030-timing):** PowerMag, "NASA Picks Three
Nuclear Power Concepts for Demonstration on the Moon" (`P3R2-D-16-S03`, fetched, 2022-06-23):
confirms the three-team structure the seed cites -- (1) Lockheed Martin + BWXT + Creare, (2)
Westinghouse + Aerojet Rocketdyne, (3) IX [Intuitive Machines/X-Energy] + Maxar + Boeing -- each
awarded $5M 12-month Phase 1 design contracts for a **40kWe** system. The requirement has since been
raised to >=100kWe per NSTM-3 (`P3R2-D-16-S01`), confirming the seed's "40kWe to >=100kWe" re-baseline
claim.

**Verdict: demand is real, current, and funded** (a directive alone would be weaker; the FY2026
appropriation is the load-bearing confirmation). This satisfies the idea's own 2028 checkpoint
precondition of "downselect/flight dates contracted" only partially -- the downselect is scheduled
(due ~April 2027) but has **not yet occurred** as of this session; the "HELD AS OPTION" framing in
the seed remains the correct posture today.

## 2. Competitors

- **Creare** (Hanover, NH) (`P3R2-D-16-S06`, fetched, creare.com): ~40 years of closed-Brayton space
  power heritage, currently developing a small-scale NASA-funded converter targeting 26% efficiency
  at 350W/164,000rpm -- confirms the seed's claim that Creare is "inside the Lockheed team" with real,
  current (not just historical) Brayton conversion work.
- **Brayton Energy LLC** (Hampton, NH) (`P3R2-D-16-S05`, fetched, braytonenergy.net): an
  **independent, merchant turbomachinery house** that explicitly "serves clients around the world,"
  has worked with 80+ clients, and has prior space-power heritage (founder's early career in
  solar-dynamic space power at Lockheed/Sanders; company built a SALT-E 35kWe gas-turbine
  alternator with Oak Ridge National Lab, per WebSearch not independently fetched this session).
  **This is a material correction to the seed's `competition_outlook_2030`:** the "merchant
  multi-team supplier position is open" framing understates the field -- an independent Brayton
  turbomachinery merchant supplier already exists and already sells across multiple government
  programs, though not confirmed yet on the FSP program specifically.
- **Rolls-Royce** (UK), via interestingengineering.com (`P3R2-D-16-S04`, fetched, 2026-02-02):
  Rolls-Royce's own lunar micro-reactor program (100kWe target, UK Space Agency-funded, £9M) has
  been **paused** after government funding expired and no launch partner materialized; the article
  also confirms **NASA and DOE have restricted FSP bidding to domestic American firms**, which
  structurally excludes Rolls-Royce (and other non-US firms) from the core US FSP competition even
  though "Rolls-Royce's US branch is still helping NASA build a converter" in some capacity. Useful
  as global technical-competitor context (Europe), not as a market.
- Westinghouse eVinci/Aerojet Rocketdyne and IX/Maxar/Boeing teams are confirmed real and current
  (`P3R2-D-16-S03`); each currently builds captive/in-house power conversion within its own team,
  consistent with the seed's "each team must reinvent it" painful-job framing.

## 3. Price signals and arithmetic

- No public per-kWe or per-unit cost figure for a space closed-Brayton PCU was found this session
  (NASA technical reports give mass/efficiency targets, not cost). **[Gap, flagged rather than
  invented.]**
- **[Assumption, wide uncertainty]:** using the $5M/team FY2022 Phase 1 study contract
  (`P3R2-D-16-S03`) and the $250M FY2026 program appropriation (`P3R2-D-16-S02`) across an
  estimated 3 concept teams plus government overhead, and assuming (order-of-magnitude judgment,
  not sourced) a power-conversion-subsystem share of roughly 15-25% of total FSP hardware value per
  typical spacecraft subsystem cost breakdowns, a plausible per-team PCU-development subcontract
  value is **on the order of $10M-$50M** across the design-to-flight lifecycle -- not a per-unit
  commercial price, since FSP is a low-volume (1-3 flight units through the early 2030s) government
  program, not a market with repeat unit sales.
- **Installed base [assumption]:** extremely small by nature -- likely 1 lunar flight unit (LR-1,
  targeted 2030) plus possibly 1-2 follow-on units (Mars/NEP demonstrations) through 2034. A merchant
  PCU supplier's revenue model here is subcontract/services-based, not volume manufacturing, which
  materially changes the venture-scale math implied by the seed's $10-30M v1 capital range -- that
  capital would need to be justified primarily by winning a subcontract position, not by unit
  economics.

## 4. Certification and export constraints

- FSP bidding is restricted to domestic American firms per NASA/DOE policy (`P3R2-D-16-S04`),
  which is favorable for a US merchant entrant (excludes foreign competition) but also means the
  addressable competitor and customer set is smaller and US-only by construction.
- Standard NASA flight-qualification (radiation, vacuum, launch-vibration, nuclear-adjacent safety
  review) applies; no idea-specific new certification barrier was identified this session beyond
  what the seed already assumes.

## 6. 2030 timing

1. **Primary/official:** NSTM-3 memorandum, 2026-04-14 (`P3R2-D-16-S01`) -- directs a >=100kWe
   reactor "ready for launch in the 2030s" and a mid-power lunar variant for 2030, with a
   downselect required within one year (by ~April 2027). Squarely inside the 2028-2035 window.
2. **Independent second (funding):** FY2026 NASA appropriations, $250M for FSP, 2026-01-05
   (`P3R2-D-16-S02`) -- confirms money is actually flowing in the founder's 2026-2029 pre-company
   window, not just policy intent.

**Verdict: window opens on schedule but has NOT yet passed the idea's own gate.** The downselect
(due ~April 2027) and any team subcontract interest are the concrete triggers to watch; until then,
studies/SBIR-only is the correct posture, exactly as the seed's FIX specifies. Program re-baselining
risk is real (40kWe -> 100kWe already happened once) and the seed's own framing of this as the
dominant risk is well-supported.
