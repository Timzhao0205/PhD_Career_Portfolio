# P3R2-A-22 — Modular plasma destruction units for concentrated PFAS waste streams (DoD / water utilities)

Analyst: demand-competitor-analyst, P4 group G02. Model/effort: Sonnet 5, high reasoning effort.
Geography rule applied: India and Singapore excluded from all market/buyer/competitor claims below.

## 0. Pre-promotion condition check

The idea's own adjudication note flags: **"EPA drivers to re-source in P4"** for the utility-side demand leg
(explicitly marked inferential in `demand_trigger_2030_2034`). This gate is now CLOSED via direct EPA sourcing
(§1, §6) — with a materially more cautious finding than the seed hoped: current EPA policy direction is toward
**delaying**, not accelerating, PFAS drinking-water compliance pressure (§6). The DoD leg of demand, by contrast, is
independently confirmed and strong (§1).

## 1. Demand

**PRIMARY buyer/procurement source:** GAO's own report (GAO-24-107322) confirms the binding statutory mandate: the
FY2020 NDAA required DoD to discontinue AFFF firefighting foam after October 1, 2024, with up to two 1-year waivers
extending the final deadline to **October 1, 2026**; ~1,500 facilities and 6,800 mobile assets are affected; DoD's own
transition cost estimate exceeds $2.1B [new:P3R2-A-22-S01]. This is a real, dated, binding government mandate that
converts directly into destruction/disposal procurement.

**Direct vendor-level confirmation of the paid precedent:** PyroGenesis (Montreal, Canada) completed a $2.25M DoD
contract (Dec 3, 2024) in which its 1MW plasma torch system treated over 300 tonnes of PFAS-contaminated material at
full capacity [new:P3R2-A-22-S02] — this independently confirms the idea's central "DoD has already paid for plasma
PFAS destruction" claim at the vendor level, with an exact dollar figure and tonnage.

**Verdict: G1 — PASS for the DoD leg; the utility leg is weaker than assumed (see §6).** The DoD-side demand is real,
dated, and already realized once at meaningful scale. The utility-side demand trigger the seed flagged as inferential
is now sourced directly to EPA and found to be **moving in the opposite direction** (regulatory delay, not
acceleration) — this should lower confidence in the utility-buyer leg specifically, while leaving the DoD leg intact.

- **374Water** (Nasdaq: SCWO, Durham, NC) — the leading SCWO (supercritical water oxidation) competitor, and

- **Aquagga, General Atomics, Revive** — additional named SCWO/hydrothermal competitors reported to be demonstrating

**China:** A direct Chinese-language search (等离子体销毁, 全氟化合物处理技术) found no named commercial Chinese

## 3. Price signals, installed base, and bottom-up arithmetic

- **PyroGenesis (weak/soft cost signal):** $2.25M contract / >300 tonnes treated implies a rough **~$7,500/tonne**
  blended cost — but this figure conflates program NRE with per-tonne variable cost and should be treated as a very
  soft upper-bound estimate, not a clean unit economics figure.
- **SCWO economics (independent, more granular):** SCWO operating costs run $0.33-$0.70/gallon depending on waste
  characteristics, with capital costs of ~$2.8M-$3.5M for a 3-4 GPM system (search-synthesized, cross-checked against
  374Water's own reported per-system annual revenue of $500k-$1.5M — consistent order of magnitude). For comparison,
  EPA-reported hazardous-waste incineration costs run $1,110-$1,610/tonne.
- **Bottom-up arithmetic (labeled assumptions):**
  - **Fact:** ~700 DoD sites have or are likely to have PFAS contamination in water or soil [new:P3R2-A-22-S01,
    cross-referenced against DoD's own 2026 interim guidance language captured in search synthesis].
  - **Assumption:** a meaningful fraction (10-30%, assumption, no source found this session) of those sites will
    require on-site or regional concentrate destruction capacity by 2030-2034, at an assumed $1M-$5M in destruction
    equipment value per site cluster (analogy to PyroGenesis's realized $2.25M contract scale).
  - This implies a DoD-side addressable equipment market of roughly **$70M-$1.05B** cumulative across the
    2030-2034 window — an intentionally wide range reflecting genuine uncertainty in both the site fraction and
    per-site equipment value assumptions.

## 4. Certification and export constraints for a 2030 launch

**Regulatory-acceptance risk, now confirmed directly from EPA:** EPA's own interim destruction/disposal guidance
formally recognizes only three "large-scale" technologies — thermal destruction (incineration), landfill, and
underground injection — and **does not list plasma or SCWO** among them; the guidance is explicitly non-binding, and
EPA committed (April 28, 2025) to updating it annually rather than the NDAA-mandated 3-year cycle
[new:P3R2-A-22-S05]. This is a direct, primary-sourced confirmation of the idea's own stated risk ("regulatory
acceptance of destruction verification methods is unsettled") — plasma destruction sits outside EPA's currently
recognized large-scale technology list, which is a real headwind for a company whose product's commercial pitch
depends on "regulator-grade" destruction-efficiency records.

No significant export-control regime applies to this product category (domestically-deployed environmental-remediation
equipment, not a controlled dual-use item).

## 5. Primary-market verdicts

- **US verdict:** DoD-side demand is REAL, dated, and buyer-reachable (a binding statutory mandate with a named paid
  precedent). Utility-side demand is weaker than the seed assumed — EPA is currently moving to delay, not accelerate,
  compliance pressure (§6) — so the addressable buyer base should be treated as DoD-led with utility demand as a slower
  secondary track, not a co-equal pillar.
- **CN verdict:** Not a market for this idea (no china_beachhead claimed) and confirmed absent as a competitor via
  direct search — no change warranted.

## 6. 2030 timing

**Timing source 1 (primary/official, dated inside 2028-2035 relevant window, though the trigger itself lands in
2026):** DoD's AFFF-transition deadline is October 1, 2026 — the final statutorily-allowed extension per the FY2020
NDAA [new:P3R2-A-22-S01]. This converts a currently-active foam-transition mandate into a medium-term (2026-2030+)
pipeline of contaminated-materials destruction procurement as ~700 flagged sites move through investigation to
remediation records of decision.

**Timing source 2 (independent, regulatory, and — critically — running counter to the seed's framing):** EPA's own
2026 proposed rules would extend the PFOA/PFOS drinking-water compliance deadline to 2031 (from April 2029) for
opt-in utilities and reopen rulemaking on four additional PFAS compounds, with a public hearing July 7, 2026
[new:P3R2-A-22-S03]. **This is a genuine, sourced downside revision to the idea's own demand-timing thesis**: the
"utility concentrate-destruction demand as PFAS treatment residuals accumulate" trigger the seed flagged as
inferential is not just unconfirmed — it is actively being pushed later by current EPA policy direction.

**Verdict: the DoD-driven window OPENS now and should PERSIST through the early 2030s** as the 700-site remediation
pipeline matures (a real, dated, statutorily-anchored trigger). **The utility-driven window is DELAYED relative to
the seed's assumption** — regulatory rollback pushes utility urgency toward 2031+ rather than the early 2030s the
seed implied. A founder pursuing this idea should weight the DoD/defense-remediation channel as the primary near-term
beachhead and treat municipal-utility demand as a slower-developing, policy-contingent secondary market.
