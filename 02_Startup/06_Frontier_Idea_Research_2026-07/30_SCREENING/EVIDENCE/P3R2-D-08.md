# P3R2-D-08 -- Demand & Competitor Evidence

**Concept:** Compact solid-state-RF-driven e-beam/X-ray sterilization machine breaking the IBA/L3
duopoly, riding the EtO phase-out.
**Analyst:** demand-competitor-analyst (Sonnet 5 / high effort), P4 group G07.
**Verdict headline: G1 = UNCERTAIN (demand exists but the specific "durable regulatory forcing
function" claim in the seed is now contradicted by a 2026 primary source; a narrower capacity/backlog
demand case still stands).**

## 1. Demand evidence

**Primary buyer/procurement source:** Sotera Health (parent of Sterigenics, the largest US
contract steriliser) Q1 2026 earnings call (fetched directly, [P3R2-D-08-S04]). CEO Michael
Petras: capex guidance **$175-225M for FY2026**; a **new X-ray-modality facility comes online
later in 2026**, with **another scheduled late 2027/early 2028**; ~$200M already spent on EtO
facility upgrades; management is "going as if the rule that's in place is gonna be the
requirement" -- i.e. capacity/X-ray-conversion investment continues independent of the regulatory
fight below. This is direct, named-buyer, dated capital-expenditure evidence of demand for
X-ray/e-beam capacity (the exact product category), not a vendor claim.

**Independent second demand source:** IBA (Ion Beam Applications, the #1 pure-play e-beam/X-ray
accelerator OEM) FY2025 results and Q1 2026 update (atlas [L05-028]; refreshed via
[P3R2-D-08-S03], fetched directly). IBA Industrial equipment backlog €218M, 2-year rolling
equipment book-to-bill 0.8x (i.e. **book-to-bill below 1 -- bookings currently trailing
shipments**, a caution flag on the "backlogged for years" framing in the original seed); IBA
launched **Rhodotron LITE**, a new low-power X-ray accelerator explicitly aimed at smaller
irradiation centres, and reports increased conference presence "notably in US and China." This
independently corroborates continued OEM-side product investment in the same capacity segment
Sotera is buying into, from the demand side (buyer capex) and supply side (incumbent OEM
still adding lower-end products, i.e. gaps the incumbent itself sees worth filling).

**Critical 2026 refresh -- this materially changes the seed's core assumption.** The seed's
`launch_2030_timing_thesis` assumption #1 required re-sourcing "the EtO forcing function is
durable regulatory pressure" from EPA/FDA primaries. That re-sourcing was done this session and
the finding cuts against the seed:

- On **March 17, 2026** EPA proposed to **repeal/relax** the 2024 EtO Commercial Sterilizer
  NESHAP rule that had been the regulatory forcing function cited by the seed (EPA press release,
  fetched directly, [P3R2-D-08-S01]). The proposal rescinds the permanent-total-enclosure
  requirement, lets facilities choose parametric monitoring over continuous emissions monitoring,
  and relaxes new-aeration-vent standards.
- SBA Office of Advocacy (fetched directly, [P3R2-D-08-S02]) quantifies the rollback's **expected
  regulatory-cost savings at $43-48M/year through 2046** -- i.e. EPA's own economic framing is that
  the 2024 rule was a meaningful cost burden being removed, not tightened.
- Comment period was extended to **May 15, 2026**; as of this writing (July 2026) no final rule
  has been confirmed reversing course, but the direction of travel is toward *less* EtO pressure,
  not more.
- FDA's Radiation Sterilization Master File Pilot Program (original 2023 program, described via
  multiple secondary sources; **not independently re-fetched this session, treat as discovery
  lead only pending a direct fda.gov fetch**) continues to exist as a parallel FDA-side incentive
  for EtO-to-radiation conversion, independent of the EPA rule fight.

**Honest reading:** the demand signal is real (Sotera and IBA are both spending real capital on
X-ray/e-beam capacity right now) but it looks more like an ongoing capacity/tort-liability/
modernization cycle than the "durable regulatory forcing function" the seed leaned on. The 2026
EPA rollback proposal is exactly the kind of mutable claim CLAUDE.md rule 18 requires refreshing,
and it removes one of the three legs the seed's timing thesis stood on. **G1 verdict: UNCERTAIN,
not FAIL** -- named buyers are spending money on the right capacity category now, but the
regulatory-durability leg specifically has flipped from "assumption to validate" to "assumption
contradicted by the most recent primary source."

## 2. Competitors

- **US:** IBA Industrial (Rhodotron accelerators; backlog/book-to-bill above, [L05-028],
  [P3R2-D-08-S03]) and L3 Applied Technologies (L3Harris ATI electron-beam systems,
  www2.l3t.com/ati/solutions/electron.htm) are the named pure-play duopoly. Sterigenics/Sotera
  and E-BEAM Services/NextBeam operate as contract-sterilisation service buyers/operators rather
  than accelerator OEMs -- do not conflate service operators with equipment competitors.
- **Europe:** IBA is Belgian (atlas geography BE, [L05-028]); no other major European e-beam
  sterilization OEM surfaced distinct from IBA in this session's searches.
- **China:** CGN Dasheng (中广核达胜) -- atlas [L05-035] (2025 half-year filing) -- operates 44
  in-house accelerators across 10 irradiation centres, describing itself as the largest
  irradiation-service operator in Asia-Pacific; new Yangzhou centre came online January 2026
  (search-derived, ccnta.cn, not independently fetched -- discovery lead). CGN Dasheng is
  **competitor/export-competition territory, not a beachhead**, consistent with the binding
  US/China geography rule and the seed's own `china_beachhead: false` flag.
- **Japan (side market):** NHV Corporation manufactures electron-beam processing systems (EPS)
  for sterilization among other applications (nhv.jp, fetched via search synthesis; no order/
  contract figures surfaced this session -- treat product-line existence as confirmed, financial
  detail as unconfirmed).
- **RF-source cost inputs:** SLAC (Adolphsen, CERN/CWRF14 presentation, indico.cern.ch) puts
  **klystron-with-power-supply cost at ~$13/W (43% efficiency)** vs **solid-state LDMOS at ~$15/W
  (35% efficiency)**, with GaN offering ~40% higher output power and ~8% better efficiency than
  LDMOS at 650 MHz (search-synthesis; direct PDF fetch returned binary/non-extractable content,
  so treat this specific $/W figure as T2/search-corroborated, not independently re-derived from
  the primary slide text this session). This is directly relevant to the 2028 FIX gate
  ("SSPA-vs-klystron cost crossover") and suggests solid-state is not yet unambiguously cheaper
  per watt than klystrons today -- the crossover the seed needs by 2028 has **not yet been
  demonstrated** in the sources found this session; GaN's efficiency edge is real but the raw
  $/W crossover claim central to the idea remains unproven.

## 3. Price signals / installed base / bottom-up arithmetic

- Minimum accelerator equipment cost for medical-device sterilization: **$1-2M**, with total
  project cost (building, shielding, controls) at least **2x equipment cost**, i.e. **$2-4M+ per
  site** (search-synthesis of vendor/trade commentary, thomasnet/mddionline-adjacent sources;
  not independently fetched -- treat as an industry rule-of-thumb, not an audited figure).
- E-BEAM Services (contract sterilizer) charges **$2,500** for initial dose-map + 3 validation
  studies, **$1,800** for standalone dose-map testing -- these are *service* fees, not evidence of
  capital-equipment pricing, and should not be conflated with the accelerator sales price
  (ebeamservices.com, search-derived).
- Varex Imaging (cargo/industrial Linatron X-ray linacs, a distinct but adjacent product line)
  FY2025 Industrial segment revenue **$77M in Q4 alone (+25% YoY)**, **$55M+ of cargo-system
  orders**, **15+ systems shipped in FY2025** (atlas [L05-033]) -- gives an order-of-magnitude
  sense of unit volumes (order-of-10-systems-per-year cadence) and per-system price
  (~$3.5-5M implied from $55M/15 units, roughly consistent with the $2-4M+ rule of thumb above,
  though Varex's cargo linacs are not sterilization units specifically -- **assumption, not a
  confirmed per-unit sterilization price**, uncertainty range $2-6M/unit).
- **Bottom-up niche arithmetic (explicit assumptions):**
  - Assume the 2030-2034 addressable US replacement/new-build pool is 15-30 sites/year needing
    new e-beam/X-ray capacity (derived from Sotera's 2 facilities/2 years cadence
    scaled loosely across the ~50 US contract sterilizers implied by "EtO sterilizes 50% of
    medical devices" [EPA press release, P3R2-D-08-S01] -- **assumption, uncertainty range
    10-40 sites/year**, no primary count of total US sterilization sites was found this session).
  - At $2-4M/unit equipment price -> **$30-120M/year total addressable US equipment spend**,
    before any share is capturable by a new entrant against an entrenched two-vendor incumbent
    base with service networks (**assumption**, wide range reflects the missing site-count
    primary).
  - A new entrant capturing even 10-20% of that (optimistic given zero installed base and no
    named LOI) implies **$3-24M/year revenue at scale** -- consistent with why the seed sizes
    V1 capital at $10-25M, i.e. this is a real but modest niche, not a venture-scale-on-its-own
    market absent the frontier_vision's distributed-appliance thesis materializing.

## 4. Certification and export constraints (2030 launch)

- ISO 11137-1:2025 (atlas [L05-042]) is the governing radiation-sterilization validation standard
  medical-device makers must requalify against; a new entrant's dose-mapping automation must be
  built to this standard out of the box, which the seed already plans.
  - FDA's Radiation Sterilization Master File Pilot Program (originally announced April 2023,
  reconfirmed as ongoing per multiple secondary sources this session, not independently
  re-fetched -- discovery lead) lowers the regulatory burden for facilities *changing* radiation
  source/method, which favors an EtO/gamma-to-e-beam/X-ray switcher, independent of the EPA NESHAP
  fight.
- Export: e-beam/X-ray sterilization accelerators are not primarily export-controlled in the way
  EUV or advanced compute is; CGN Dasheng's China buildout (atlas [L05-035]) and Varex's own China
  exposure context (IPG's unrelated China competition disclosure aside) suggest this is a
  globally-traded capital-equipment category with no confirmed US export-control barrier found
  this session. **No dedicated export-control primary source was located or is claimed here; this
  is an absence-of-evidence statement, not a confirmed "no restrictions" finding.**

## 5. Primary-market verdicts

- **US: demand real, buyer reachable, window plausible but narrower than the seed claims.**
  Sotera Health and IBA are both spending real capital now on the right capacity category
  ([P3R2-D-08-S04], [P3R2-D-08-S03], [L05-028]); the buyer set (contract sterilizers, device OEMs)
  is identifiable and reachable via existing sales channels IBA/L3 already use. However, the EPA
  2026 rollback proposal materially weakens the "regulatory forcing function" framing; the demand
  case must be re-argued as capacity/backlog/liability-driven, not regulation-driven, going into
  P4/P6 scoring.
- **China: competitor territory, not a US-founder beachhead**, consistent with `china_beachhead:
  false` in the seed. CGN Dasheng is entrenched, state-linked, and the binding geography rule
  excludes China as a beachhead for this idea regardless.
- **Window (2030):** Real 2026-2028 activity (Sotera's two new facilities, IBA's Rhodotron LITE)
  suggests the capacity-expansion window is open *now* through the near term, which could mean it
  is already being filled by incumbents before a 2030 startup could enter -- a timing risk the
  seed's own `timing_window_risk` field already flags ("If incumbent backlogs clear by 2029...").
  This session's evidence sharpens that risk: IBA's book-to-bill is already below 1.0, and
  Sotera's own capex is proceeding "regardless" of the regulatory outcome, meaning incumbents are
  not waiting for a forcing function to invest -- they are already closing the capacity gap the
  seed is counting on being open in 2030.

## 6. 2030 timing sources (two independent, >=1 primary/official 2028-2035 trigger)

1. **Primary/official:** Sotera Health Q1 2026 earnings call ([P3R2-D-08-S04]) -- named,
   dated capacity build: X-ray facility online late 2026, second facility **late
   2027/early 2028**. This is a company-official (CEO/CFO on-record) trigger inside the
   2028-2035 window required by the mission rubric.
2. **Independent second source:** EPA reconsideration proposal timeline ([P3R2-D-08-S01],
   [P3R2-D-08-S02]) -- comment period closed May 15, 2026; a final rule (in either direction)
   is a distinct, dated regulatory event that will land before 2030 and will determine whether
   the "regulatory forcing function" leg of the thesis is real or not. This is an official-source
   trigger, though it points toward *removing* pressure rather than adding it, which is the
   opposite direction the seed assumed.

**Window verdict:** Narrows/uncertain by 2030, not clearly open. The capacity-expansion window
is open now (2026-2028) and may be substantially filled by incumbents (Sotera, IBA) before a
new entrant could reach volume production; the regulatory tailwind the seed counted on for
2030-2034 is actively being reversed as of March-May 2026. A 2030 entrant should expect to compete
on cost/footprint against incumbents who already converted, not ride an open regulatory door.
