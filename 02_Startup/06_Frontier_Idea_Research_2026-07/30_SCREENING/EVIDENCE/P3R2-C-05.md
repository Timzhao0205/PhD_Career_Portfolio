## 1. Demand (per market leg)

### US leg
- **Primary (buyer specification):** New source **P3R2-C-05-S01** (Nidec, fetched, `company_filing`, T1):
  confirms Google **open-sourced a concrete, dated hardware specification** ("Project Deschutes" CDU) to
  "selected and certified vendors" -- 2MW-class cooling capacity, 80 PSI, low approach-temperature design,
  with an IEEE-519 ultra-low-harmonic-distortion VFD requirement. This is a genuine buyer (Google)
  authoring a vendor-facing spec, exactly the kind of primary demand evidence this task requires -- and
  critically, the spec is a *hardware* spec, not an *acceptance-test/qualification* standard, which is the
  gap C-05 targets.
- **Independent second:** New source **P3R2-C-05-S02** (Data Center Dynamics, fetched, `trade_press`, T2):
  independently confirms eight named vendors (Boyd, CoolerMaster, Delta, Envicool, Nidec, nVent, Stulz,
  Vertiv) built to the same spec, with matching figures (2MW, 3°C approach temperature, 80 PSI) -- a clean
  cross-check on S01.
- **Verdict: demand is real.** Multiple named, funded vendors are building to a buyer's spec with no
  independent conformance-test standard behind it -- precisely the "spec chaos" C-05 monetizes.

### CN leg
- **Primary:** Atlas L14-039 (T/CIEP 0263-2025, China liquid-cooling technical specification, `standard`,
  T2) and new source **P3R2-C-04-S02** (NDRC/MIIT action plan, shared with C-04, fetched, government, T1):
  PUE ≤1.25/1.2 mandates create acceptance-testing stakes for CN data center operators.
- **Independent second:** Atlas L14-035 (GB 40879, `standard`, T1). Also relevant: this session confirmed
  (via search, not independently fetched) that **China Taier Laboratory (泰尔实验室)**, part of CAICT
  (China Academy of Information and Communications Technology), performs CDU/liquid-cooling certification
  testing in China -- a concrete existing certification-channel target consistent with the idea's
  "sell-to-certification-labs" extension (merged from B-02).
- **Verdict: demand is real** -- China's mandatory PUE/liquid-cooling standards create conformance-testing
  obligations with an identifiable institutional buyer channel (CAICT/Taier), though C-05 would need to
  independently verify Taier's specific CDU test-bench gaps before assuming a sales channel exists.

**G1: PASS (both legs).**

## 2. Competitors

- **US:** No dominant *merchant* thermal-test-vehicle/rack-emulator vendor was identified this session --
  consistent with the idea's own claim. Component-level thermal test chip/vehicle suppliers exist (e.g.,
  Taiwan's PTC/KLC heater-based TTV products, found via search, not independently fetched -- these serve
  component-level thermal characterization, not the rack-scale/2MW calorimetry-grade acceptance testing
  C-05 targets). OCP itself publishes a "Cold Plate Development and Qualification" white paper (found via
  search, opencompute.org, not independently fetched this session due to repeated 403 responses from that
  domain) -- this is a **competitive/substitution risk worth flagging**: if OCP's own working group
  publishes a full qualification methodology (not just a hardware spec), C-05's product-market fit narrows
  to being an implementation of OCP's method rather than an independent one.
- **China:** Envicool and other Deschutes-compliant vendors (S02) are potential customers, not competitors,
  for a metrology product; CAICT/Taier Lab is an institutional adjacent-capability holder, not (on current
  evidence) a merchant competitor.
- **Japan/Taiwan:** Atlas L14-050 (NTT Facilities verification hub, Japan) and atlas L14-053 (ITRI, Taiwan)
  remain the closest institutional analogs identified across this and the prior P3 pass -- both are
  research/verification facilities, not product companies, consistent with the idea's own framing.
- **Overall:** the "no dominant merchant vendor" claim held up under this session's checks, with the
  caveat that OCP's own standards process (not a company) is the single biggest structural risk to the
  differentiation.

## 4. Certification and export constraints

- No JEDEC liquid-cooling-specific test standard was found in development this session (existing JEDEC

  acceptance testing) -- consistent with the idea's "no standard test hardware" claim for the US leg.
  ASHRAE TC 90.4 compliance was mentioned in search results as a real, adjacent requirement for PUE
  validation (not independently fetched) -- worth checking directly in P5/P6.
- Export control: test/measurement equipment is broadly uncontrolled; no evidence found of export
  restriction risk for this category, consistent with the idea's "dual-entity trivial" framing.

## 6. 2030 timing (two independent sources)

1. **Primary/official:** Atlas L14-035 (GB 40879 revision, China, `standard`, T1) -- ongoing revision to
   extend scope to liquid cooling, with a 2025-2027 implementation cadence feeding into 2030-era enforcement.
2. **Buyer-side:** New source S01/S02 (Google Project Deschutes spec, 2025, `company_filing`/`trade_press`)
   -- establishes that the current buyer-driven hardware-spec cycle is already underway in 2025, with vendor
   qualification demand following immediately (2026 SC25 exhibits) rather than waiting for 2030. This is a
   **near-term (2026-2028), not 2030-2034, demand window** for the core test-equipment product -- the 2030
   framing in the idea record understates how soon the metrology gap is already biting.

**Verdict:** the window is **already open (2026-2028)** on the US buyer-spec side and **opening through
2025-2027** on the CN mandate side; both should **persist** past 2030 as chip TDP and cooling-mode diversity
continue to outpace any single standard, consistent with the idea's small-but-durable "picks and shovels"
framing. The main timing risk is not "too early" (as the idea's 2030-2034 trigger language implies) but
"already live" -- a first-mover advantage may be more time-sensitive than the idea record's 2028+ pilot
timeline assumes.
