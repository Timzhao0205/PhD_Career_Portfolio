# P3R2-D-09 -- Demand & Competitor Evidence

**Concept:** Charge-buildup-immune beam current/dose metrology for FLASH and industrial e-beam --
a non-intercepting monitor cross-calibrated against Faraday/calorimetric reference.
**Analyst:** demand-competitor-analyst (Sonnet 5 / high effort), P4 group G07.
**Verdict headline: G1 = PASS (narrow but real niche; buyer and problem both independently
confirmed; market is small and instrument-vendor-shaped, as the seed itself admits).**

## 1. Demand evidence

**Primary buyer/procurement source:** NIH/NCI SBIR Contract Topic 461, "Ultra-Fast Dose Rate
(FLASH) Radiation Detectors and Safety Systems for Cancer Treatment" (fetched directly,
[P3R2-D-09-S01], sbir.cancer.gov). This is a **federal contract solicitation**, not a grant --
Phase I up to $400K/12 months, Phase II up to $2M/2 years, 2-3 anticipated awards, explicit
technical bar of measuring dose rates from **2-10 Gy/minute to 40-120 Gy/second**, dose-delivery
monitoring within 5% tolerance, **mandatory NIST traceability**, and time-structure/pulse-vs-
continuous discrimination -- i.e. the federal government is a direct buyer of exactly the
instrument category D-09 proposes. Caveat: the specific solicitation instance found is dated
(deadline November 14, 2023, part of PHS-2023-1); this NCI SBIR contract topic list rotates
annually and this specific instance is expired, but it establishes that NCI has bought this exact
capability once already via a federal contract vehicle (as opposed to a grant), which is stronger
demand evidence than a grant would be, and the underlying clinical need (documented below) has not
gone away. A verifier should check PHS-2026-1/2027-1 topic lists for a live recurrence before
final scoring.

**Independent second demand source:** Two independent 2025 peer-reviewed papers (already in atlas,
[L05-023] Renaud/Muir/Williams/McEwen, Medical Physics 2025, National Research Council Canada;
[L05-024] Bernelin/Muir/Renaud et al., Medical Physics 2025, Canada/France) both document that
standard beam-current transformers are **corrupted by electric-field-driven charge buildup at
FLASH dose rates** -- i.e. two independent research groups, not the idea's own team, have
published the exact unsolved calibration problem the product addresses. This is technical-
literature confirmation of the painful job, distinct from (and reinforcing) the NCI buyer signal.

**Named commercial buyer-side evidence:** IntraOp Medical's Mobetron with FLASH-IQ reached its
**10th worldwide installation by May 2023** (businesswire, search-derived, not independently
re-fetched this session -- treat as T2/discovery-supported), and PTW's flashDiamond UHDR detector
is already deployed at **12+ European labs/hospitals** but explicitly labeled "for research use
only" (fetched directly, [P3R2-D-09-S02], ptwdosimetry.com) -- i.e. the clinical FLASH device base
is real but still small (order of 10-20 sites globally), and even the leading dosimetry incumbent
has not yet turned its UHDR detector into a commercial (non-research) product. This confirms the
seed's own honest framing: a real, documented gap, but a small near-term installed base.

## 2. Competitors

- **US/incumbent instrumentation:** Bergoz Instrumentation (atlas [L05-049], France but sells into
  the US/global accelerator market) -- NPCT beam-current transformers, published price list,
  **EUR 44,950 (22.2mm ID) to EUR 113,900 (198.4mm ID)** per unit (search-synthesis of GMW
  Associates' resale listing of Bergoz's own price list; not independently re-fetched this
  session, treat as approximate/dated 2020-2022 list pricing). Bergoz is optimized for storage
  rings/steady beams, not UHDR transients -- exactly the gap the seed claims.
- **Europe:** PTW-Freiburg (Germany) -- flashDiamond UHDR detector, developed with University of
  Rome Tor Vergata, already the closest named incumbent competitor building specifically for
  FLASH dosimetry, currently positioned as a relative-dosimetry research tool, not yet a
  reference-grade traceable instrument ([P3R2-D-09-S02]). IBA Dosimetry is named in search results
  as active in this space but no specific FLASH product was independently confirmed this session
  (discovery lead only).
- **Clinical device makers (the indirect "customer" for calibration services):** IntraOp Medical
  (US, Mobetron), THERYQ/PMB-ALCEN (France, VHEE 100-200 MeV compact linac, partnered with CERN
  and CHUV per search-derived CERN Courier coverage, not independently fetched this session).
- **Japan (side market):** QST (Quantum Science and Technology Institute) is running FLASH
  radiobiology research (qst.go.jp, fetched via search synthesis) but no dedicated beam-metrology
  product or procurement was found; treat as a research-partner lead, not a buyer.
- No China-based FLASH/UHDR metrology competitor or buyer was identified in this session's
  searches; this idea is consistent with the seed's `china_beachhead: false` and does not need a
  China-market claim.

## 3. Price signals / installed base / bottom-up arithmetic

- Bergoz NPCT unit price range **EUR ~45K-114K** (see above) is the closest available price anchor
  for a "drop-in form-factor" competing instrument; the seed's product explicitly targets this
  exact replacement category.
- **Bottom-up niche arithmetic (explicit assumptions):**
  - Clinical FLASH sites: ~10-20 installed globally today (IntraOp's 10th unit as of 2023, plus
    THERYQ/CHUV and other academic UHDR linac conversions) -> **assumption: 30-60 clinical sites
    worldwide needing traceable UHDR dosimetry by 2030-2032**, uncertainty range wide given the
    field's early stage (no primary count of global UHDR-capable clinical beams was found).
  - Industrial e-beam sterilization sites needing ISO-11137-1:2025-compliant dosimetry: informed
    by the D-08 evidence file's installed-base discussion (IBA/L3 duopoly, CGN Dasheng in China) --
    **assumption: 50-150 industrial e-beam plants globally are candidates for an upgraded
    calibration-certificate product**, most of which do not currently need UHDR-specific
    metrology (conventional e-beam sterilization runs below FLASH dose rates), so this leg is
    smaller than it first appears; **assumption, uncertainty range 20-60 realistic near-term
    targets**.
  - At an instrument price of **$50-150K/unit** (using Bergoz's EUR range as an anchor, converted
    and rounded) and even optimistic 30-50% penetration of the ~30-60 clinical sites by
    2032-2034, this implies **$0.5-4.5M cumulative equipment revenue** from the clinical leg alone
    over several years -- a small, instrument-company-scale market, consistent with the seed's
    own `timing_window_risk` admission that "industrial e-beam alone caps the market at tens of
    millions." The NCI SBIR Phase I/II ($400K + $2M) is a plausible non-dilutive bridge to first
    revenue, not a scale-defining contract.

## 4. Certification and export constraints (2030 launch)

- **NIST traceability is an explicit, mandatory requirement** in the NCI SBIR solicitation itself
  ([P3R2-D-09-S01]) -- this is the calibration-chain standard the product must meet, not an
  optional nice-to-have; this substantially de-risks the "what does 'good' look like"
  specification question for a 2026-2029 pre-company plan.
- ISO 11137-1:2025 (atlas [L05-042]) governs the sterilization-QA product tier absorbed from A-08.
- No export-control barrier was identified for beam-diagnostics instrumentation in this session's
  searches; this is a scientific-instrument category, not obviously covered by dual-use export
  regimes, though a verifier should check EAR/ITAR classification for any accelerator-specific
  components before a 2030 international sales plan (absence of evidence, not a confirmed
  "no restriction" finding).

## 5. Primary-market verdicts

- **US: demand real, buyer reachable, window plausible.** NCI is a real, identifiable federal
  buyer with a contract vehicle (not just a grant) for exactly this instrument category
  ([P3R2-D-09-S01]); clinical FLASH developers (IntraOp) and academic UHDR beam facilities are
  reachable via existing accelerator-physics conference/collaboration channels, consistent with
  the seed's own precompany plan (university medical-physics collaborations).
- **China: not assessed as a beachhead** -- no China-specific buyer, competitor, or demand trigger
  was found or claimed; consistent with the seed's `china_beachhead: false`.
- **Window:** Narrow but durable. This is not a fast-closing window (FLASH clinical translation is
  a multi-year academic-to-clinical process, not a single procurement cycle), and the underlying
  physics problem (charge-buildup corruption of BCTs) does not resolve itself -- it will still be
  true in 2030 unless someone builds the fix. The risk is market-size ceiling, not window closure.

## 6. 2030 timing sources (two independent, >=1 primary/official 2028-2035 trigger)

1. **Primary/official:** NIH/NCI SBIR Contract Topic 461 ([P3R2-D-09-S01]) -- establishes NCI as
   a recurring federal buyer for FLASH detector/safety-system capability; even though this
   specific 2023-cycle instance has an expired deadline, it demonstrates the government
   acquisition vehicle exists and has been used once already, with FLASH clinical trials
   (following [L05-023]/[L05-024]'s documented instrumentation gap) continuing to scale toward
   2028-2032 regulatory-qualification pressure per the seed's own thesis.
2. **Independent second source:** DOE HEP Accelerator Stewardship Awards (atlas [L05-031]) --
   14 FY2024 awards spanning national labs, universities, and companies in accelerator/ultrafast-
   laser R&D, showing continuity of US federal funding for exactly this technical community
   through the mid-2020s, a leading indicator that beam-instrumentation R&D funding (of which a
   NIST-traceable UHDR monitor would be a natural fit) remains active going into the 2028-2030
   window.

**Window verdict: opens/persists through 2030 for a narrow, instrument-company-scale niche.**
Not a fast-closing window, but also not a large one; the seed's own "tens of millions" market-cap
admission is corroborated, not contradicted, by this session's evidence.
