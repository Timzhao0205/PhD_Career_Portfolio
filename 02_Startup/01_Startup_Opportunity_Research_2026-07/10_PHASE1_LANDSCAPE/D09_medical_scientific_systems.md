# D09 — Medical & Scientific Systems: Compact NMR/MRI Subsystems, RF/Microwave Power, Gradient Amplifiers, Scientific Power Supplies

## Landscape

Four adjacent hardware stacks make up this domain: (1) benchtop/low-field NMR and MRI (permanent-magnet or cryogen-free HTS magnets, gradient drivers, RF transceivers); (2) medical RF/microwave power (RF ablation generators 300–500 kHz, microwave ablation 902 MHz/2.45 GHz, and RF/LLRF power for proton/particle-therapy accelerators, 60–700 MHz, kW–100s of kW); (3) gradient amplifiers for MRI and beam-steering magnets; (4) precision scientific/lab power supplies (programmable DC, high-voltage). The benchtop NMR market is small and mature (~$150M in 2023 to ~$250M by 2033, 5.3% CAGR) [D09-01], dominated by Bruker (Magnettech/microESR), Oxford Instruments, Nanalysis, and Magritek, with new instruments priced roughly $30K–$150K depending on field strength [D09-02]. Gradient amplifiers and RF amplifiers for clinical MRI are a captive OEM component market supplied almost exclusively by Prodrive Technologies (NL), which does not sell single units outside large-volume OEM programs, plus legacy in-house divisions of GE/Siemens/Philips/United Imaging [D09-23]. RF/microwave ablation generators sit inside a $4.3B (2022) to a projected $10.4B (2032) device market, with RF generators alone at ~$2.9B in 2022 revenue [D09-03], increasingly built on GaN-on-SiC solid-state modules (RFHIC, Stellant, Elite RF) replacing magnetrons [D09-04][D09-21]. Particle-therapy RF power is a small, high-value, high-reliability niche: solid-state modular amplifiers (tens of kW at VHF, hundreds of kW aggregate) are displacing klystrons in new cyclotron/synchrocyclotron designs because of efficiency, graceful degradation, and footprint [D09-06][D09-22]. China's push into compact superconducting synchrocyclotrons (2.2 m diameter, <50 t) has cut proton-center build time from 3–5 years to 1–2 years [D09-07].

## Pain points & buyers

Academic/industrial NMR and EPR labs want higher sensitivity and multinuclear capability in a benchtop footprint without paying for superconducting-magnet infrastructure or helium logistics; buyers are university PI equipment budgets and QC/QA labs in pharma, food, and oil/gas. MRI OEMs and their Tier-1 integrators (United Imaging, Siemens, GE, Philips, plus China's second tier: Chenguang Medical/辰光医疗, Ningbo Jianxin/宁波健信, Teshen/特深电气) are single- or dual-sourced on gradient amplifiers and want a credible alternative supplier with shorter lead times and lower unit cost, since most Chinese MRI makers besides United Imaging still buy upstream core components rather than build them in-house [D09-08]. Interventional-oncology device companies (Medtronic, Boston Scientific, J&J MedTech, AngioDynamics) buy RF/microwave generator subsystems or license GaN modules to cut generator size and heat while adding real-time impedance/temperature feedback; the buyer is the systems-integration team inside a $10.4B RF-ablation device market, not a component-only startup customer, unless the startup sells directly as an OEM subsystem supplier [D09-03]. Particle-therapy OEMs (IBA, Varian, Mevion, and China's Apstrong/CGN/中科离子) and national labs building next-generation compact cyclotrons need modular, field-serviceable solid-state RF amplifiers because tube/klystron supply chains are shrinking and unplanned tube replacement/downtime is a large pain point for hospital-operated systems [D09-06][D09-22]. Scientific/lab power-supply buyers are physics/EE labs, semiconductor test houses, and instrument OEMs who want higher power density, programmability, and lower noise than commodity bench supplies, in a market Credence estimates at $500M (2024) growing to $834M (2032) for precision high-voltage supplies alone [D09-09].

## Incumbents & gaps

Bruker BioSpin (NMR/EPR/preclinical imaging) reported a $2.57B backlog across all four Bruker segments at end of 2025 and explicitly flags reduced US/China research funding, tariffs, and supply-chain constraints as risks — signaling both incumbent strength and vulnerability to funding-cycle downturns that a lean, low-overhead competitor could exploit in specific niches [D09-10]. Gradient amplifiers are structurally a captive OEM component: Prodrive will not sell single units outside large-volume OEM contracts, a real gap for the growing compact/low-field and academic-scanner segment (Halbach-array, permanent-magnet portable MRI) — one published academic workaround repurposed a $1,200 modified audio power amplifier as a 3-axis gradient driver for small permanent-magnet systems, versus proprietary units costing far more, illustrating both the price umbrella incumbents enjoy and how thin the technical moat is for low-power/low-field use cases [D09-11][D09-23]. In particle-therapy RF, published solid-state amplifier work (20 kW-class at 83.2 MHz, ~68–71% efficiency) still comes mostly out of national labs and university groups (SKKU/SKKUCY-10 program) rather than being productized into an off-the-shelf modular catalog part; RFHIC and Stellant sell components/modules but full turn-key cyclotron RF systems remain bespoke, engineering-heavy contracts [D09-05][D09-22][D09-04]. Ultra-compact HTS/REBCO magnets for brain-only 1.5T MRI (Robinson Research Institute, NIH-funded, <400 mm bore, 14 km REBCO tape, cryogen-free) show the physics is achievable at small size but the team hit real manufacturing failure modes (epoxy-encapsulation cracking under thermal gradient) that no commercial vendor has yet solved at production scale — a genuine opening for someone with real winding/packaging expertise [D09-12].

## 2029 inflections

FDA already treats MRI systems from 0.064T to 7T identically under the Class II, 510(k)/substantial-equivalence pathway (21 CFR 892.1000), so there is no special low-field regulatory tax — a low-field or benchtop hardware company can get to market on the same track used for prior clearances [D09-13][D09-14]. REBCO/HTS tape costs continue to fall on the back of fusion-driven demand, which by 2029 should make cryogen-free HTS compact-MRI and compact-cyclotron magnets meaningfully cheaper than today's early prototypes — a founder with genuine no-insulation HTS winding experience is unusually well positioned to catch this curve. GaN-on-SiC RF/microwave devices are on a steep growth trajectory that pulls ablation and accelerator RF sources toward fully solid-state, field-replaceable modules and away from magnetrons/klystrons, a shift already visible in review literature and product catalogs [D09-15][D09-21]. China's 14th Five-Year Plan allocated 41 heavy-ion/proton systems nationally plus 8 more (Feb 2024) reserved for private hospitals, and China now counts 30+ proton centers operational/under construction — over half of all new global proton projects — a durable, policy-backed demand wave for accelerator subsystems including RF power through the early 2030s [D09-17][D09-07].

## China notes

CIQTEK (国仪量子), a University of Science and Technology of China spinout founded 2016, has built a genuine multi-product scientific-instrument company (benchtop EPR EPR200M, benchtop NMR CAN400/600, NV-diamond quantum sensing, electron microscopes) and is now filing for an IPO on the Shanghai STAR Market (科创板) [D09-18][D09-19], evidence that a compact-instrument hardware company can scale from zero to public-listing in under a decade in China with strong technical-university backing. On MRI hardware, China has real independent suppliers below the United Imaging tier — Chenguang Medical (辰光医疗, ~20 years in RF coils/magnets/gradient coils, Philips supplier), Ningbo Jianxin (宁波健信, largest independent domestic magnet maker, preparing IPO), and Teshen Electronics (特深电气, RF/gradient coils and amplifiers, sole Chinese GE technology-award winner) — all candidates for component partnership, competition, or acquisition-target intelligence [D09-08]. On proton therapy, China now has five active domestic system developers (Shanghai Apstrong, China General Nuclear Technology/中广核技, Maisten Medical/迈胜医疗, Institute of Modern Physics/CAS, HUST), a state-mandated demand pipeline, and a stated strategic goal of full domestic supply-chain control for high-end radiotherapy equipment, which raises both a market opportunity (subsystem supply to these five) and political risk (a US-based hardware supplier may be excluded once "国产化" mandates tighten) [D09-16][D09-17].

## Opportunity seeds

**Modular solid-state RF/LLRF amplifier cassettes for compact proton/carbon-ion cyclotrons.** Pain: klystron/tube supply chains are shrinking and unplanned tube failures cause costly treatment downtime at hospital-operated centers; who pays: cyclotron OEMs (IBA, Mevion, China General Nuclear Technology, Institute of Modern Physics/CAS) and national labs building next-gen compact synchrocyclotrons. Extreme performance (GaN-on-SiC power density, graceful N+1 module redundancy, fast field-swap serviceability) directly reduces clinical downtime, a metric hospitals will pay a premium for. A 2–5 person team can prototype a single ~1–5 kW VHF module and combiner stage — the published academic building blocks already exist at 68–71% efficiency — without needing a cleanroom, then sell into the OEM/national-lab prototype stage before scaling to full systems [D09-05][D09-06][D09-22]. China's mandated 41+8-unit proton/heavy-ion buildout and the founder's Chinese network make this a credible dual-market entry via CGN/中科离子 relationships alongside US/EU cyclotron builders.

**Cryogen-free, field-serviceable compact HTS magnet subsystems for benchtop MRI/NMR.** Pain: existing ultra-compact 1.5T HTS prototypes hit real manufacturing failure modes (epoxy cracking under thermal cycling) that block productization; who pays: MRI startups, university medical-physics departments, and veterinary/industrial NMR OEMs who want higher field than permanent magnets without liquid helium [D09-12]. The founder's undergraduate no-insulation HTS winding automation is direct, differentiated capability few teams have; a tiny team can iterate winding/packaging faster than an academic lab or a large incumbent's slow internal magnet division. Falling REBCO tape costs (fusion-driven) make this economics-favorable by 2029; China's HTS tape supply base and the US DOE-funded HTS ecosystem both offer sourcing optionality.

**GaN-native modular RF/microwave generator platform for interventional ablation OEMs.** Pain: legacy ablation generators still carry magnetron-era size, heat, and coarse-control limitations; who pays: mid-tier interventional-oncology device companies and Chinese ablation device makers who need a licensable GaN RF engine rather than building one in-house, inside a market growing from $4.3B to $10.4B [D09-03][D09-04][D09-21]. Extreme performance in fast start/stop switching, fine energy-delivery control, and thermal density lets a small team win a subsystem design-in before a device maker commits to full acquisition. A 2–5 person team can build and qualify a single-channel GaN generator reference design without FDA-class clinical-trial burden if sold as an OEM subsystem, since regulatory risk sits with the device integrator. Both US and China ablation markets are growing at high single/low double digits, giving two independent sales paths.

**Low-cost precision gradient-amplifier module for the emerging low-field/portable MRI segment.** Pain: Prodrive and legacy OEM gradient amps are sold only in large-volume clinical contracts, locking out the growing low-field, portable, and academic-scanner segment that needs a modest three-axis driver, not a six-figure clinical-grade unit; who pays: portable/low-field MRI startups, academic Halbach-array labs, and veterinary/small-animal imaging OEMs [D09-23]. Extreme performance here means precision current control and low thermal drift at a fraction of clinical-grade cost — a gap the published $1,200 audio-amplifier hack proves is real but under-engineered [D09-11]. A 2–5 person power-electronics team with GaN/SiC and precision-analog background (the founder's core skill set) can out-execute this niche fast, since the physics and control loops are well documented in the literature. No China-specific regulatory barrier exists at the low-field end (same FDA 510(k) track) [D09-13][D09-14], so the same module can be sold to both US and Chinese low-field MRI integrators.

**Precision programmable scientific power supply line for compact-accelerator and quantum-instrument labs.** Pain: physics/EE labs and instrument OEMs (accelerator magnets, quantum-sensing hardware, compact-cyclotron test benches) need high power-density, low-noise, tightly regulated DC/HV supplies beyond commodity bench units, in a precision-HV-supply market growing from $500M (2024) toward $834M (2032) [D09-09]. Buyers are national labs, university accelerator groups, and instrument-OEM integrators (including CIQTEK-scale China players) who currently piece together supplies from multiple vendors. A small team with deep power-electronics and precision-analog expertise can differentiate on noise floor, transient response, and programmability rather than competing on catalog breadth, and can sell direct to labs without any medical-device regulatory pathway at all — the cleanest, lowest-regulatory-risk seed in this domain, with both US national-lab and Chinese instrument-maker (CIQTEK-adjacent) channels open [D09-18][D09-19].

**Compact, cryogen-free NMR relaxometry/spectrometer engine for industrial (non-diagnostic) markets.** Pain: benchtop NMR users in oil/gas (core analysis), food/agriculture, and battery-electrolyte QC want higher sensitivity than permanent-magnet 60–100 MHz units without superconducting-magnet cost; who pays: industrial QC labs and instrument integrators in a benchtop-NMR market growing modestly from $150M to $250M by 2033 [D09-01][D09-02]. A small team combining precision RF instrumentation and compact-magnet packaging can push field strength/SNR past today's permanent-magnet ceiling toward a cryogen-free HTS-assisted design, entering as a component/engine supplier to existing instrument brands rather than building a full go-to-market analytical-chemistry sales channel from scratch — deliberately avoiding the diagnostic/clinical path and the transformer-monitoring space entirely [D09-08].
