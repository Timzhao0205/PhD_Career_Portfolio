# D03 — Fusion & Big-Science Supply Chain

## Landscape

Private fusion is shifting from lab science to industrial procurement. FIA's 2025 supply-chain survey (22 private companies, 57 suppliers) found global fusion supply-chain spend grew 73% in 2024 to $434M and was projected to grow another 25% in 2025 to $543M, with suppliers investing $230M of their own capital in new capacity [D03-15][D03-02]. Yet 83% of suppliers still see fusion as a business risk and 81% cite lack of demand certainty as the barrier to scaling — a classic chicken-and-egg problem that favors sellers of standardized, reusable subsystems over one-off custom builds [D03-02]. Component categories buyers report as most critical: magnets (7 of 20 companies surveyed), precision manufacturing (31% worried now, 63% worried about future capacity), power supplies, cryogenics, and vacuum systems [D03-15][D03-02].

Magnets alone are ~28-40% of tokamak capital cost, and toroidal-field coils were modeled as the single largest line item (~$1.5B of a $3.1B tokamak cost estimate in one NASEM-adjacent analysis) — meaning even small percentage efficiency gains in magnet-adjacent hardware (power supplies, protection, winding) are worth real money to a buyer [D03-09]. Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS) is now selling HTS magnets to competitors (Realta Fusion, Type One Energy, University of Wisconsin's WHAM) rather than waiting for its own reactor revenue, explicitly modeling itself as fusion's "ASML" [D03-01][D03-05]. Tokamak Energy spun out TE Magnetics to sell HTS magnet systems into adjacent markets (data-center power distribution, electric aviation motors, maglev, submarine MHD propulsion, particle accelerators) and won a £70M UK STEP program magnet-systems contract (March 2026–March 2029) [D03-08][D03-12]. National Academies flags a structural US gap: America pioneered REBCO coated-conductor manufacturing ~15 years ago but lost the industrial base; SPARC alone needs 10,000-20,000 km of 4mm REBCO tape, largely sourced overseas today [D03-09].

## Pain points & buyers

- **Precision manufacturing capacity is the top near-term bottleneck.** 31% of fusion companies worry about it today, rising to 63% for future needs [D03-02]. Buyers are the ~50 private fusion companies (CFS, Type One, Realta, Xcimer, Proxima, Tokamak Energy, plus China's Energy Singularity, Startorus, ENN, CFEC) and national labs/ITER-adjacent programs.
- **Magnet power supplies and pulsed converters** are a named critical category; DOE's Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program ($46M committed of $415M authorized through FY2027, 8 companies) is pushing awardees toward late-2020s preliminary design reviews, creating a wave of procurement for pulsed power, ECRH/ICRH, and coil power hardware in 2027-2029 [D03-13].
- **Splice/joint reliability in HTS magnets** — National Academies explicitly names tape splices as the weakest point in coils, a recurring failure mode buyers must design around [D03-09].
- **Vacuum vessel and structural fabrication** is capital-intensive and slow (Westinghouse's $180M ITER vacuum-vessel welding contract with Ansaldo/Walter Tosto illustrates the scale and multi-year timelines of incumbent big-metal suppliers) [D03-11][D03-19] — not a fit for a small team.
- **Chinese buyers are moving in parallel and fast**: Hefei Institute of Plasma Physics (CAS) alone tendered ¥1.345B across four tritium-fuel-cycle subsystems in a single November 2025 batch [D03-07]; China's state-backed "Spark-1" (星火一号) commercial demonstrator has an estimated ¥4-5B magnet-system order pipeline running 2026-2029, with the whole project exceeding ¥10B [inferred from D03 China searches, not fetched — flag for Phase 2 verification].

## Incumbents & gaps

Magnet manufacturing is increasingly vertically integrated by the reactor builders themselves: CFS and Tokamak Energy both build and now sell magnets, meaning a new entrant competes with well-funded incumbents that treat magnets as strategic IP, not a commodity buy [D03-01][D03-08]. Power-supply incumbents (OCEM Power Electronics, CERN-adjacent suppliers) serve accelerators and fusion with thyristor/IGBT converters up to 50kA and 5ppm stability, but are decades-old, custom-quote, low-volume shops — no evidence of GaN/SiC-native, compact, rapid-turnaround competitors [D03-14]. Gyrotron/RF heating is a four-company global oligopoly (CPI, GYCOM, Thales, Canon/CETD) serving fusion and defense/radar; extremely high barriers (vacuum electronics fab, decades of tube expertise) make this a poor fit for a small team [D03-18]. Vacuum vessel and large-structure fabrication is dominated by heavy-industry primes (Westinghouse, Ansaldo, Hyundai Heavy Industries) — also a poor fit [D03-11][D03-19]. The clearest structural gaps for a small extreme-performance team sit one layer down from the "hero" magnet/heating systems: precision winding/fabrication tooling, protection electronics, and compact power conversion — categories buyers name as bottlenecks but where no dominant modern supplier has emerged [D03-02][D03-09].

## 2029 inflections

DOE's Milestone Program authorizes funding through FY2027 with awardees targeting preliminary fusion-pilot-plant design reviews by the late 2020s [D03-13] — procurement for magnet, power, and protection subsystems will peak just as the founder's PhD ends (~2029). China's Spark-1 hybrid fusion-fission demonstrator targets 2029 construction completion with a ¥40-50B cumulative magnet order pipeline [China searches, unverified — Phase 2 to confirm with primary filing]; CAS/Hefei institute tenders are already running at >¥1B per batch and accelerating [D03-07]. UK STEP program contracts (Tokamak Energy magnet partner, £70M through March 2029) show Western public fusion programs converging on the same window [D03-12]. National Academies' call to "double" US HTS wire investment within 2-3 years (i.e., by ~2027-2028) signals a US industrial-base push that a US-based hardware supplier could ride [D03-09].

## China notes

China's approach is state-anchored and fast: China Fusion Energy Co. was capitalized at ¥11.5B by seven central enterprises in mid-2025 to lead engineering commercialization, alongside private entrants (Energy Singularity/能量奇点, Startorus/星环聚能, ENN). Energy Singularity's HH70 — the first fully-HTS tokamak — reported 95% domestic sourcing of materials and is now raising $500M for a next-generation HH170 by 2027, explicitly citing China's HTS supply-chain strength as a competitive edge over better-funded US rivals [D03-10]. Domestic listed suppliers span the full stack: Western Superconducting (低温超导线, LTS wire, 2025 fusion revenue +140%), Yongtai (永鼎, 2G HTS tape, Q1 2026 profit +474%), Baosheng (宝胜, superconducting cable, >80% market share, ¥520M 2025 contract), LianChuang Optoelectronics (联创光电, HTS magnet coils, won the ¥1.8B+ Spark-1 magnet-system award), and Bingren Environment (冰轮环境, cryogenics) [D03-06]. Reported domestic-localization rate for core fusion equipment now exceeds 96% [China search synthesis — verify primary source in Phase 2]. This is both a threat (China's magnet/cable/cryo layer is arguably ahead of the US on manufacturing scale) and an opportunity (a founder with native Chinese and real networks could sell into this pipeline directly, or supply the layer China is not yet dominant in — precision power electronics and protection).

## Opportunity seeds

1. **Compact GaN/SiC pulsed magnet power converter modules.** Pain: fusion buyers name magnet power supplies as a critical, capacity-constrained category, and legacy suppliers like OCEM run decades-old thyristor/IGBT topologies quoted per custom project [D03-14][D03-02]. Buyer: fusion pilot-plant builders (CFS competitors, DOE Milestone awardees, Chinese private/state programs) and the broader accelerator/proton-therapy market as a hedge. A GaN/SiC-native, power-dense, fast-response converter directly matches the founder's power-electronics and wide-bandgap fabrication background and needs no cleanroom fab of magnets themselves. A 2-5 person team can build and bench-test converter modules without needing a reactor partner, selling to both US pilot plants and Chinese tenders (CAS institutes alone tendered >¥1.3B in one batch) [D03-07].

2. **Fast solid-state HTS quench-protection dump switches (protection hardware, not monitoring).** Pain: National Academies and recent Nature Scientific Reports work confirm REBCO magnets have slow normal-zone propagation and GJ-scale stored energy, making protection switching a genuine unsolved hardware problem — Oxford Sigma/STEP just demonstrated a new protection concept in 2025 [D03-09][D03-21][D03-20]. Buyer: every HTS magnet integrator (CFS, Tokamak Energy, LianChuang, Western Superconducting) needs a protection subsystem, not a monitoring dashboard — this is a standalone safety-critical power component, sidestepping the condition-monitoring/diagnostics space. Extreme performance (sub-millisecond, high-current solid-state switching) plays directly to SiC/GaN device design skill. A small team can prototype and qualify a switch module without building a magnet; sell into both fusion and the larger MRI/motor HTS market as a hedge.

3. **Automated precision no-insulation HTS coil-winding and QC tooling.** Pain: splices and winding-property uniformity are named failure points, and China's magnet suppliers are scaling fabs fast (LianChuang's ¥1.8B+ order, Western Superconducting's 140% revenue growth) which implies a wave of new winding-line capex [D03-09][D03-06]. Buyer: magnet manufacturers building new production lines (both US entrants scaling from prototype to pilot-plant volumes, and Chinese suppliers under tender pressure). This is a direct extension of the founder's undergraduate laser-aligned automated winding machine — a capital-equipment sale (machine tools, not a reactor), sellable to both US and Chinese customers, and does not compete with CFS/Tokamak Energy's own magnet IP since it's the tool, not the coil.

4. **Low-resistance HTS current leads and joint/splice modules.** Pain: splices are explicitly flagged as the weakest point of NI-HTS coils; this is a component problem, not a systems problem, and precision manufacturing capacity is the #1 bottleneck cited by 63% of fusion companies for future needs [D03-09][D03-02]. Buyer: magnet integrators globally, plus the broader superconducting-magnet market (MRI, high-field research magnets). A tiny team with applied-superconductivity and packaging expertise can iterate joint designs on a bench rig without reactor access, then qualify with one or two magnet-builder customers — low capital intensity, high technical differentiation.

5. **Compact conduction-cooled cryostats/test dewars for HTS coil R&D and QC.** Pain: cryogenic system suppliers (Leybold, Edwards, Chart) serve large industrial/semiconductor cryo, not the smaller, higher-precision test-stand niche that magnet fabs and university programs need for coil qualification runs [D03-06 industry-map informed]. Buyer: magnet manufacturers and national-lab/university magnet programs running QC before shipping coils to reactor builders. Extreme performance (low heat leak, fast turnaround, laser-aligned fixturing) matches the founder's harsh-environment packaging and applied-superconductivity background; sellable as a bench-scale product independent of any single fusion company's roadmap, in both US and China markets.

**Dead ends noted for Phase 2:** gyrotron/RF heating tubes (four-company global oligopoly, decades of vacuum-electronics know-how, poor fit) [D03-18]; vacuum-vessel/large-structure fabrication (heavy-industry primes only, $100M+ contracts) [D03-11][D03-19]; full magnet-system manufacturing (CFS/Tokamak Energy/LianChuang are vertically integrating and treat this as core IP) [D03-01][D03-08][D03-06].
