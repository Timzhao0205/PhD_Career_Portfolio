## 1. Title

Thermal-contraction-matched (or compliant-pressure-maintained) turn-to-turn interlayer
for a conduction-cooled, no-insulation high-temperature-superconducting winding, and
method of making the same.

## 2. Field

Cryogen-free / conduction-cooled high-temperature-superconducting (HTS) components:
specifically the turn-to-turn interface of a no-insulation (NI) or metal-insulation (MI)
coated-conductor winding operated with limited cryocooler cooling power and no liquid
cryogen bath. Anchor application: conduction-cooled NI field coils (e.g. compact
aviation-class machines and cryogen-free MRI/NMR inserts).

## 3. Problem / Background

In an NI HTS winding the turn-to-turn interface is a single physical layer performing
two coupled jobs: it sets the turn-to-turn contact resistance Rc (which governs charging
delay, current-sharing, and field quality) and it sets the radial thermal-conductance
path that drains local heat toward the cold head. In cryogen-free operation there is no
liquid bath to buffer transients; the machine is repeatedly cooled down and warmed up
over its service life (cryocooler duty cycles, maintenance, off-states).

The prior art already establishes, and thereby forecloses as a genus, several nearby
propositions:

- **Engineered NI interlayers that control Rc, characterized under cycling, are known.**
  US 11,282,624 B2 (FSU/NHMFL, in force) discloses resistive coatings (Cu-oxide, Cr, Ni,
  Ni-P) and Ni/Cu-plated stainless interlayers selected to *engineer* Rc, and reports
  experimental thermal-cycling (4.2 K↔273 K) and mechanical/pressure-cycling
  (~1000 cycles, 2.5–25 MPa) Rc data. **This candidate does not claim that genus.**
- **CTE-mismatch delamination at the NI interface is a named, published failure mode,
  with a published mitigation.** The MIT/Iwasa-Hahn-lineage conductive-epoxy impregnation
  NPL (ledger T3-6) names thermal-contraction-mismatch delamination explicitly and
  teaches a *partial-depth impregnation* geometry fix. Merely "recognizing and mitigating
  CTE mismatch at the NI interface" is therefore not novel.
- **Dual-function (Rc + thermal) engineered interlayers under conduction cooling are
  published.** The soldered-metal-insulation (SMI) NPL (ledger T3-4, IEEE #9769961) engineers
  both Rc and thermal conductivity and tests under conduction cooling below 77 K.
- **Compliant / spring-loaded contact maintenance across cooldown at a cryocooler joint
  is old art.** US 4,930,318 (Belleville-washer cold-head interface) and US 5,701,742
  (compliant indium gasket) anticipate the *generic* mechanical concept — so any compliant
  variant here must be turn-to-turn-of-an-NI-winding specific.

**What is NOT found in the searched art (the surviving gap):** a turn-to-turn interlayer
whose *integrated thermal contraction* (293 K → operating T) is itself the engineered
design variable — matched to the effective contraction of the coated-conductor stack, or
held by a per-turn compliant element maintaining contact pressure across cooldown — for
the **joint** purpose of bounding **both** Rc drift **and** turn-to-turn
thermal-conductance drift over a specified number of cryocooler thermal cycles.
US 11,282,624 *characterizes* cycling but does not *design the interlayer against it via
CTE matching*; T3-6 solves CTE mismatch by a geometry fix for Ic retention, not by a
matched-material rule co-controlling Rc and thermal-conductance drift.

## 4. Summary

A conduction-cooled NI (or MI) HTS winding is provided in which the turn-to-turn
interlayer is engineered specifically around its thermal-contraction behavior: either
(a) the interlayer material/stack is selected so that its integrated thermal contraction
over 293 K → operating temperature matches, within a specified tolerance, the effective
integrated contraction of the coated-conductor turn stack; and/or (b) a per-turn
compliant element maintains turn-to-turn contact pressure above a specified floor across
cooldown and repeated cycling. The engineered CTE-match (or maintained contact pressure)
is used as the design variable that keeps **both** the turn-to-turn contact resistance
Rc **and** the turn-to-turn thermal conductance within specified bounds over a specified
number of cryocooler thermal cycles. A method of making claim covers winding-time
selection and verification of the interlayer against the integrated-contraction
tolerance.

The distinction over the closest in-force reference (US 11,282,624 B2) is deliberate and
narrow: that reference selects/engineers the interlayer for its *Rc value* and merely
*observes* cycling behavior; here the interlayer is selected for its *contraction match
(or maintained contact pressure)*, and that selection is the mechanism that *bounds*
drift. See §8 for the explicit exclusion of the genus and §7 for the FTO distinction.

## 5. Independent Claim (combination — ladder rung 1)

The claim elements below are centered on **CTE-matching / compliant contact-pressure
maintenance as the designed variable** — deliberately not on a generic "engineered
interlayer," which is US 11,282,624's territory.

**Claim 1.** A conduction-cooled superconducting winding, comprising:

- **(a)** a no-insulation or metal-insulation coated-conductor winding having a plurality
  of turns, adjacent turns defining a turn-to-turn interface;

- **(b)** a cold head thermally coupled to the winding and configured to remove heat by
  solid conduction under a limited cooling-power budget, with no liquid-cryogen bath in
  thermal contact with the winding;

- **(c)** a turn-to-turn interlayer disposed at said interface, wherein the interlayer is
  **selected such that the magnitude of the difference between (i) the integrated thermal
  contraction of the interlayer over the range from 293 K to the winding operating
  temperature and (ii) the effective integrated thermal contraction of the coated-conductor
  turn stack over the same range is less than a specified tolerance** — i.e. the
  interlayer's thermal-contraction match to the turn stack is the engineered property that
  defines the interface;

- **(d)** wherein, by virtue of said contraction match, the turn-to-turn contact resistance
  Rc of the interface remains within a first specified bound over a specified number N of
  cryocooler thermal cycles between the operating temperature and a warm state; **and**

- **(e)** wherein, by virtue of said contraction match, the turn-to-turn thermal conductance
  of the interface remains within a second specified bound over said N cryocooler thermal
  cycles, such that a peak hot-spot temperature rise of the winding under the limited
  cooling-power budget stays within a current-sharing thermal margin.

The "specified tolerance" of element (c) is intended to be expressed **functionally and
measurably** (an integrated-contraction tolerance, testable at winding time), rather than
as a bare "matched" adjective — this is the enabling-rule content (ladder rung 3) folded
into the independent claim so that elements (d)–(e) have an operative antecedent.

**Note on elements (d)/(e):** these are drafted as *bounds maintained*, not as specific
numeric cycle-life or k-floor windows. Numeric parameter-window claims are **omitted
entirely** (see §5.1) because the simulation does not yet support any criticality-based
numeric range (G-PHYS=REVISE; see §9).

## 6. Dependent Claims

**Claim 2 (compliant-element variant — ladder rung 2; drafted as an explicit design-around
of US 11,282,624 for FTO purposes).** The winding of claim 1, wherein the turn-to-turn
interlayer comprises a per-turn compliant element (for example a co-wound wavy or
resilient metallic strip, or an elastic conductive interlayer) that maintains the
turn-to-turn contact pressure above a specified floor across cooldown and across said N
cycles, such that Rc is set and bounded **by maintained mechanical contact pressure rather
than by a resistive coating composition** engineered for an Rc value. This variant is
recited specifically so that the Rc-control mechanism does **not** read on US 11,282,624's

the generic cryocooler-joint precedents (US 4,930,318 / US 5,701,742), the compliant

**Claim 3 (radial-CTE-grading variant — ladder rung 4).** The winding of claim 1, wherein
the integrated thermal contraction of the interlayer is **graded radially** from the
winding inner diameter to the outer diameter to track the radial hoop-stress / contraction
profile of the winding, such that the contraction-match tolerance of element (c) is met
across the radial extent of the coil. (No reference in the searched ledger discloses radial
CTE grading of an NI turn-to-turn interlayer.)

**Claim 4 (method of making).** A method of making the winding of claim 1, comprising, at

contraction over 293 K → operating temperature is within said specified tolerance of the
effective integrated contraction of the coated-conductor turn stack; and verifying said
tolerance during or after winding (for example by a witness-coupon contraction measurement
or an in-process contact-pressure measurement).

**Claim 5 (dual-bound co-control — dependent emphasis).** The winding of claim 1, wherein a
single contact-quality state of the interface simultaneously sets both the Rc of element
(d) and the thermal conductance of element (e), such that the contraction match bounds both
responses through one physical contact interface rather than through two independently
engineered layers.

**Claim 6 (application-field limitation — ladder rung 6; weak, retained for CN utility model

## 7. Embodiments

- **CTE-matched interlayer (primary).** A metallic or composite interlayer whose composition
  and thickness are chosen so its integrated 293 K→T_op contraction matches the effective
  contraction of the REBCO/Hastelloy/Cu tape stack within the element-(c) tolerance,
  co-wound continuously between turns and providing a continuous radial thermal drain toward
  the cold head.

- **Compliant per-turn element (Claim 2).** A co-wound resilient metallic strip (e.g. a
  wavy/undulating thin metal) or an elastic conductive interlayer whose spring-back holds
  contact pressure above a floor across cooldown; Rc is set by maintained pressure, giving an
  Rc-control mechanism outside US 11,282,624's resistive-coating claims.

- **Radially graded interlayer (Claim 3).** Interlayer contraction property varied ID→OD
  (e.g. by graded composition or graded thickness) to track the hoop-stress/contraction
  profile.

- **Winding-time verification (Claim 4).** Witness-coupon integrated-contraction measurement
  and/or in-process contact-pressure trimming per turn or per azimuth.

- **Operating regime.** Conduction-cooled by a cryocooler cold head at a fixed limited
  cooling-power budget (illustrative cold-head temperatures 20 K / 30 K / 50 K), no cryogen
  bath.

## 9. Prophetic Examples

> **All quantities in this section are SIMULATED / prophetic (present tense), from
> `20_SIM/cf6_contraction_matched.py` and `20_SIM/out/cf6_starter.json`. They are NOT
> measured data and are NOT blended with any measured data. They are illustrative of a
> hypothesized mechanism only. Two serious problems with these numbers are stated

**Prophetic Example A (present tense, SIMULATED).** A conduction-cooled NI pancake with a
uniform turn-to-turn interlayer is modeled with a radial 1-D conduction network
(`thermal_solve()`), a local hot-spot heat input of 0.5 W at turn 20, and a cold-head sink
at the OD. The interlayer thermal conductance is degraded over thermal-cycle count N by an
**assumed** exponential decay proxy k(N) = k_floor + (k0 − k_floor)·exp(−N/τ), with
k0 = 400 W/m·K. A "CTE-matched" interface is assigned a higher floor (k_floor = 150 W/m·K)
and slower decay (τ = 3000 cycles); a "CTE-mismatched" interface is assigned a lower floor
(k_floor = 15 W/m·K) and faster decay (τ = 400 cycles). After N = 10,000 cycles the
projected peak hot-spot ΔT **rise** is 0.0915 K (matched) versus 1.549 K (mismatched) — a
factor of ~17. These are prophetic outputs of an assumed parametrization, not measured
results.

### 9.1 PROBLEM 1 — the simulation provides ZERO evidence for the cryogen-free-specific framing (the candidate's entire thesis)

The projected peak-ΔT rise is **numerically identical across all three tested cooling
budgets**: peak_dT_rise_matched = 0.09154607… K and peak_dT_rise_mismatched = 1.54887919…
K at cold-head temperatures of **20 K (G_ch=1 W/K), 30 K (G_ch=4 W/K), and 50 K
(G_ch=20 W/K) alike** (identical to ~10 significant figures in `cf6_starter.json`). In this
linear radial model the cooling-budget term only shifts a baseline offset; the
degradation-driven *rise* is set entirely by the radial network and the assumed k(N). **A
bath-cooled coil with the same radial topology would show the same rise.** Therefore the
candidate's differentiating claim — that cryocooler duty cycling under *limited* cooling
power is a worse environment than a bath, and that this is why the CTE match matters — is
**asserted, not simulated.** As currently built, the simulation is not evidence for the
cryogen-free-specific hook at all. This must be fixed (see §10) before the cryogen-free
framing is credible.

### 9.2 PROBLEM 2 — the model contradicts the closest MEASURED literature

The simulation assumes **universal monotonic degradation** of contact quality with cycling.
This directly contradicts real measured data in the single closest in-force reference:
**US 11,282,624 B2 reports that mechanical/pressure cycling (~1000 cycles, 2.5–25 MPa)
DROPPED Rc to ~1/10 of its initial value** — i.e. contact quality *improved* (bedding-in)
under that cycling mode, opposite in sign to this sim's assumption. (The same reference's
*thermal* cycling 4.2 K↔273 K raised Rc modestly, ~0.8→3.1 µΩ·cm², consistent with
degradation — so the sign of the effect is mode-dependent in the real data.) **The sim's
monotonic-decay proxy is therefore not a universal cycling law, and presenting it as one is
directly contradicted by measured art.** At most it is defensible only for
thermal-cycling-driven gap-opening, and even that must be reconciled with, or explicitly
distinguished from, US 11,282,624's measured pressure-cycling result before any reliance.

### 9.3 The ~17x headline is an input, not a finding

The ~17× ratio (1.549 / 0.0915) is a **direct arithmetic consequence of the assumed inputs**
— the hand-picked 10× ratio of k_floor values (150 vs 15 W/m·K) and the ratio of τ (3000 vs
400 cycles). The thermal solve merely translates assumed k into ΔT. This number is honestly
labeled as prophetic in the source, so it is not a candor violation, but **it is not
validated evidence of criticality** and **no quantitative or parameter-window claim rests on
it** (hence §5.1). It must not be presented anywhere as an independently established result.

### 9.4 Electrical half is unmodeled

CF-6 claims the interface bounds **both** Rc and thermal conductance, yet the electrical
contact resistivity in the sim is a frozen placeholder (`rho_ct_placeholder`, unused by the
thermal solve). The electrical half of the invention currently has **no simulation support**.
