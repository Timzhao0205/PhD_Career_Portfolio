# G03 — Ocean & Marine Electrification

## Landscape
Four sub-markets share one buyer profile: capital-constrained, regulation-driven, not tech-constrained. Wave/tidal PTO conversion is pre-commercial — CorPower's C4 runs at Aguçadoura, Portugal; China's Zhoushan LHD is the only tidal plant with 8+ continuous grid-tied years [G03-07][G03-16]. Shore- and vessel-side charging for workboats, tugs, and ferries is scaling fast (Port of LA, Xiamen, new ferry routes) [G03-03][G03-09][G03-01]. Offshore stationary power for buoys, seabed nodes, and floating charging barges is served by bespoke, quote-only vendors [G03-11][G03-05]. Port/island/aquaculture microgrids form a ~$1.15B (2025), ~7%/yr market dominated by propulsion-bundling OEMs (ABB, Siemens Energy, Wärtsilä, Corvus) [G03-10]. EU AFIR mandates TEN-T shore power by 31 Dec 2029 [G03-18]; China tightened shore-power rules in May 2025 [G03-06]. Vessel-side electrification is outrunning shore/offshore infrastructure everywhere — the gap is widest away from the crowded propulsion drivetrain (C01/C03 territory).

## Pains & buyers
Port authorities must fund double-digit-MW connections — cruise berths need up to 16MW continuous, container berths ~7.5MW, versus the 1-2MW already used for cranes, against a 2029 deadline with only ~20% of required EU connections installed or contracted by mid-2025 (cruise 38%, container 11%), and Italy alone has budgeted EUR 700M [G03-12]. Shipowners must fund receiving/conversion gear: $50k-$2M per vessel by power class [G03-08], or ~RMB 800,000 (~$110k) per MW in China [G03-14] — costly enough that only 5-6% of Chinese dry-bulk/container ships carried compatible gear as of 2021, holding national shore-power usage at 17% despite 73-100% berth coverage [G03-14]. Offshore-wind, aquaculture, and marine-science operators need persistent power for buoys and workboats away from grid connection, but incumbents (Verlume, Ocean Power Technologies) sell custom systems with no public pricing [G03-11]. Wave/tidal OEMs need certified conversion modules; even CorPower, the sector leader, bespoke-commissions a "SiC-inverter system" from Equipmake rather than buying off a shelf [G03-07] — proof no catalog product exists yet.

## Incumbents & gaps
- **Shore power (HVSC >1MVA)**: standardized under IEC/IEEE 80005-1 (2019, amended 2021/2023) [G03-17], built by ABB/Siemens/Wärtsilä-class integrators bundling transformers, converters, and switchgear at multi-MW scale. No compact, fast-deploy product exists for ports below cruise/container scale.
- **Workboat DC charging**: an unstandardized power range. Baumüller's manual CCS2 stations reach 425kW/plug, automatic crane-fed stations reach 4MW, system bandwidth tops 5.6MW [G03-13]; Motive Energy's Port of LA build for Curtin Maritime pairs four 1MW DC chargers with a 10MWh BESS [G03-03]. Nothing standardized fills the 200kW-1MW tugboat/crew-transfer band.
- **Wave PTO conversion**: one pace-setter — CorPower (C4 live, C5 in production, 10MW VianaWave and 5MW EMEC arrays backed by EUR 17.5M EIC, EUR 40M EU Innovation Fund, EUR 19M Horizon Europe in 2025) [G03-07] — bespoke-commissioning its power electronics rather than buying a qualified product. No supplier serves smaller PTO developers.
- **Offshore stationary/buoy power**: Verlume's Halo subsea batteries (35-55kWh, to 3,000m, 260Wh/L, quote-only) [G03-11] and Bilfinger/Schneider Electric's NUI buoy (wind+solar+battery+diesel, no umbilical, 1,000 autonomous hours, up to 50% lower remote-asset cost) [G03-05] prove the concept; an offshore-construction power barge (75MWh, $9.5M capex, $0.10/kWh input vs. $0.30/kWh vessel-equivalent rate, serving multiple vessels 240 days/yr) [G03-04] proves the economics. Each is bespoke, not a repeatable SKU.

## 2029 inflections
EU AFIR Article 9(1) forces 90%-of-port-calls shore power at TEN-T ports by 31 Dec 2029 [G03-18]; at ~20% installed by mid-2025 [G03-12], 2027-2029 is the buildout crunch. DOE-funded PacWave begins the first-ever wave-generated electricity deliveries to the US grid under a Bonneville Power Administration PPA running 2026-2030 [G03-02] — first revenue-grade performance data for WEC power conversion. CorPower's 10MW VianaWave and 5MW EMEC arrays land in this same window [G03-07] — the first moment multiple PTOs need mass-produced, not bespoke, power electronics. US federal hydropower-plus-marine-energy appropriations hit $220M in FY2026, the highest ever [G03-02], with TEAMER alone funding 39 marine-energy projects ($6.3M) [G03-02] — non-dilutive runway for a 2029-launch startup.

## China notes
MOT Order 2025 No.2 (26 May 2025) tightened shore-power rules — mandatory parameter confirmation and public disclosure by ports/suppliers/vessels, oversight assigned to Yangtze and Pearl River maritime administrations [G03-06] — a compliance-instrumentation opening, not just hardware. The 2017 Layout Plan's targets (50%+ of specialized berths across 69 major ports, 493 berths) [G03-15] are structurally met (73% coastal, ~100% inland coverage), yet 2020 usage sat at 17% [G03-14] — the world's clearest documented "built but unused" shore-power case; one Jinzhou berth sat idle 18 months [G03-14]. Zhoushan's LHD project (3.4MW modular tidal units, State Grid-connected, 8+ years continuous, 7.83M kWh cumulative) [G03-16] is the longest-running grid-tied tidal plant globally — a credibility anchor for pitching PTO electronics into Chinese demonstration programs. Xiamen just commissioned China's largest new-energy tugboat fleet (four vessels, 6,300-7,300kW hybrid power, 4,400-5,000kWh batteries) [G03-09] — fast-growing charging demand at a port with no dedicated MW-class tug-charging system yet announced.

## Opportunity seeds
1. **Berthed-load shore-tie frequency/voltage converter.** A compact GaN/SiC AC-DC-AC retrofit unit lets any vessel accept shore power regardless of onboard frequency/voltage, priced to beat China's ~RMB 800k/MW benchmark [G03-14] and close the 73%-coverage/17%-utilization gap. Buyer is the shipowner or port authority, not a shipyard. Engines and propulsion motors sit idle at berth, so this stays outside **C01/C03** (marine-USV/propulsion power bricks) — different buyer, different category: a stationary hotel-load interconnect, not a drivetrain inverter.

2. **Modular offshore charging barge for CTVs and workboats.** A containerized battery-plus-power-conversion barge, sized like the $9.5M/75MWh offshore-construction case [G03-04], serves offshore-wind O&M fleets and aquaculture platforms away from grid connection. This avoids **C04** (MCS truck-charging modules): it is a marine-native, salt-fog and wave-duty-cycle design sold to offshore-wind operators and aquaculture farms, not a repackaged highway-depot megawatt-charging standard.

3. **Qualified PTO-to-grid conversion module for WEC/tidal OEMs.** A ruggedized, pre-certified grid-interface converter for variable-speed, low-speed PTO generators, sold as a component so WEC/tidal developers stop bespoke-commissioning electronics per project as CorPower currently does [G03-07]. This avoids **N04** (subsea inductive power & docking couplers): energy flows out to the grid from a harvesting device, not into a resident AUV/ROV — opposite direction, different buyer.

4. **Catalog-priced power/comms pod for fixed ocean instruments.** A standardized, publicly-priced battery-plus-power-conditioning-plus-comms module for scientific buoys, met masts, and aquaculture sensor nodes undercuts Verlume/OPT's custom quote-only model [G03-11]. Target buyers are oceanography institutes, met agencies, and aquaculture operators who now wait weeks for a bespoke quote. This also avoids **N04**: it serves fixed or periodically-serviced installations, not mobile AUV/ROV charging docks.

5. **Grid-forming microgrid controller for port/island/aquaculture platforms.** A power-conditioning and control package integrates shore-tie, local solar/battery, and hotel loads for small ports, islands, and aquaculture ranches — the fastest-growing microgrid segment [G03-10]. This avoids **PWR-13** (MVDC electric-ship power & protection system): the product serves land- or platform-based shore/island/farm loads, not a ship's underway MVDC distribution bus — different location, different buyer.
